# The Last Unread Mark

> The watchers spoke. They did not say they had conquered hidden things. They said their seeing had outrun their singing.

I made the quadrant for the observers before any mouth called the work fearful.

It was cedar from the north store, dry through three summers. I rubbed ash into the grain so the sighting cuts would hold. I laid seven crossmarks for the seven nights of checking, and I tied the cord with red thread at the length used for roof beams, field rows, and graves.

The singers had asked for it. The old lamp song no longer kept the sowing days close enough. Children were planting by verses their grandmothers had trusted, and the beans came late. No elder accused the sky. No observer accused the singers. A song may grow tired where many mouths have carried it. A quadrant may rest the song's feet.

That was the purpose I wrote on the back: for the care of the song.

On the first night we climbed the old rise. The observers stood in their appointed places. I held the cord low so it would not drink wind. The red star came where the song said it would come. The pale star followed, but its lower rim stood a thumb-width beyond the third cut.

I blamed the quadrant.

At dawn I planed the edge again and set it against the straight beam in the storehouse. I hung the cord by the hearth and found no wetness in it. I asked the observer with the strongest sight whether sleep had weighed on his face. He answered by washing his eyes and not by taking offense.

On the second night the pale star stood the same thumb-width beyond the third cut.

On the third night we changed the quadrant. On the fourth we changed the rise and let another observer call the cuts before I touched the record. On the fifth we laid every tool on the ground and looked with closed mouths, saying nothing that might teach the stars a new road.

The pale star did not return to the sung place.

In the record I wrote same error.

I sat with the stylus over the clay until the mark dried. Then I wet my thumb, smoothed the word error, and wrote answer in its place. The observer beside me saw the change. He did not bless it. He did not forbid it. His hands were under his robe, and I could hear the cord knots knocking together where they hung from his belt.

On the sixth night one observer wept. He did not weep loudly. He held his mouth shut as if a cry might move another star. I wanted to put bread in his hand. Instead I counted the cuts again, because my own hands had forgotten every other mercy.

On the seventh night no one read aloud.

We descended with dust on our knees. I carried the quadrant wrapped in cloth. The observers carried the cord, the wet record, and the broken place in the song. We washed before the common house. The water took the dust. It did not take the answer.

The elders set bread beside us first.

Some said later this was pardon. Some said it was warning, because bread placed first may mark the one who must not be touched until judgment has cooled. I ate, because my mouth was dry and because the elder watched to see whether I would refuse the people's bread.

The quadrant lay in the center under cloth. No child had been called, but the doorways were full of children's breath. The singers stood behind them. The undertakers stood near the wall, hands gray with ash from names they had warmed before dawn.

The oldest elder asked what we had done.

I said, "We measured as we were taught."

The elder waited.

I said, "We measured again."

The elder waited longer.

Then one observer, whose tears had dried white on his cheek, said, "Our seeing has outrun our singing."

No one struck him. That was worse than striking. If the word had been wicked, a rod could have found it. If the word had been foolish, laughter could have shut it. The word stood in the house and did not leave.

The marks were covered. The observers were wrapped in sleeping cloth. Their eyelids were washed with warm water and ash. Salt was placed on their tongues. I was not wrapped, because I had not been appointed to see. I sat awake with the quadrant across my knees while the singers kept the old lamps in their low voices.

At evening the houses spoke warm names. I heard them through the walls: seed, hearth, salt, door, mother, blood, morning, soil. I knew then that a quadrant could bruise more than wood.

When the observers woke, they ate. They thanked those who had covered them. Their eyes were lowered.

Then a child asked whether the lost lamp had found its house.

Every observer turned. I turned too. The quadrant shifted on my knees and the last cut struck my thumb hard enough to raise blood.

The council lasted seven nights, or else it lasted the length of time a people needs before fear learns to sit without a knife. The surveyors sat apart by our own request. Our cords and quadrants were not broken, but no hand rested on them without flinching.

The singers spoke of a line that changed while they sang it. The undertakers spoke of graves whose names answered late. The children asked whether looking had harmed what was looked at. No answer came quickly enough to be a shield.

On the last night the elders placed three things in the center: the measuring cord, a covered lamp, and a bowl of soil from the oldest field. They told us none would rule alone.

Measure without song would become cruelty.

Song without measure would become refusal.

Soil without either would be loved and lost.

After this, new rules were given. No observer would stand alone. No mark would be kept in one house only. No report would be spoken without naming the hands and mouths that had stood beside it. Children would learn the lamp names, but only after warm names. Undertakers would warm the cooled names more often, because the dead must not be made to carry what the living feared to hold.

I copied the quadrant three times. One copy went to the singers. One went to the undertakers. One went to the house where the children learned seed counts.

The first quadrant I carried back to the rise.

I did not go alone. A singer came with a cracked voice. A child came because the elders said the wound hidden from children becomes their inheritance without a name. An undertaker came with ash under his nails and a bowl of old soil in his hand.

We stood until the red star appeared. We stood until the pale star leaned where it had leaned before. I set the cord between the cuts. I read the first mark. I read the second. I read the third, and the singer repeated each one after me as if numbers could be kept inside a song if given enough breath.

At the last mark I stopped.

The child looked at me. The singer did not urge me. The undertaker held the soil bowl level.

I could have read it. My eyes were clear. The cut was clean. The cord lay straight.

I left the last mark unread.

Not because the star would move if named. Not because the number was forbidden. Not because I was merciful.

I left it because the people had no house yet for that mark. A hand may lift more than it can carry. A mouth may open before there is bread enough for every listener. A quadrant may hold a cut that a people has not yet learned to bear.

So I wrapped the quadrant before dawn. The last mark remained under cloth, not hidden from truth, but waiting for the form that could carry it without breaking the song, the grave, the soil, and the child who would someday ask why I had stopped.

## Translator's Note

This witness uses the word rendered as "measure" for the cord, the quadrant, the act of reading, and the communal permission to let a reading count. I have kept the overlap. Separating tool from rite would make the passage cleaner and less accurate.

The last mark is not necessarily suppressed data. It may be an unread value, an unperformed declaration, or a withheld state change. The manuscript lets the maker appear cautious, but I cannot exclude the possibility that the maker's refusal produced the very communal form it claims to await.

I have translated "seeing" and "reading" differently where the surface permits it. The source may not distinguish them.
