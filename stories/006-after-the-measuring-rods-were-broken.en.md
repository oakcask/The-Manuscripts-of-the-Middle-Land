# After the Measuring Rods Were Broken

> The later singers say the rods were broken so that no elder would mistake the old breadth of a field for the breadth required by the ship. The operator's copy gives a colder instruction: after the rods are broken, do not measure against them again.

Before the common fire was lit, I checked the input tables for the last time.

Soil from each boundary. Ash from each hearth. Water from each well. A little dark from each closed room. Warm names from the mouths that still answered. Cooled names from the burial gardens. Refusals, where a hand had struck away the bowl. Oaths, where two enemies had sworn to be carried without forgiving.

All were written in one reference order.

The order troubled the younger keepers. They wanted the holy things apart from the dirty things, the names apart from the water, the vows apart from the refusals. I told them to leave the marks in the order by which they could answer one another.

"A road that refuses the grave will not know where to turn," I said.

This was not wisdom. It was the rule by which I had been taught. What is not placed in the reference order cannot be sought after departure. What is sought after departure and has no place of reference becomes a cry in the hull.

At sunset the singers began to teach the people the gentler words.

Not breaking. Folding.

Not exile. Bearing.

Not death, if the sleeper could be kept.

I did not correct them. A hand can perform a true task while the mouth is given a kinder word for it. Still, I kept my own input list beside my knee and touched each mark with a dry reed.

Soil. Ash. Water. Dark. Warm name. Cooled name. Refusal. Oath.

When the moon rose, the measuring rods were brought out.

They had hung in the meeting house longer than any living elder had served. Their ends were darkened by palms. Notches ran along them for house-width, grave-depth, roof-right, marriage debt, water turn, road tax, and the space that must remain between two quarrelsome doors.

The boundary wardens held them up for blessing.

The singers thanked them. I had helped write those lines, and I knew where each thanks had been placed. A rod must be honored before it is put away, or those who trusted it will think their lives have been mocked. A rod must be broken before the folding begins, or some hand, frightened by the narrowing of a room, will reach for the old length and command the house to return.

The first rod broke with a clean sound.

Several people cried out. One woman covered her ears and then uncovered them, ashamed of needing both gestures. A boy near the fire whispered that the west field would not know its edge.

I marked the break.

The second rod splintered. That was ill. A clean break can be set in the fire as one witness. A splintered break produces many small witnesses, and small witnesses are eager to disagree. I ordered the pieces gathered under one cloth before any singer could turn the accident into comfort.

"Do not name the splinters," I said.

The youngest keeper stared at me.

"Not even to mourn them?"

"Not until the house has narrowed."

He obeyed. He was young enough to think obedience would keep his hands clean.

After the rods were broken, no old measure and no old reference line were permitted to touch the work. We used cord that had not yet measured field or wall. We used the span from wrist to throat, because every living worker carried that measure already and could lose it only by ceasing to answer. We used the delay between one breath and the next. We used the time a name needed to leave the mouth and return from soil.

Then the calling began.

Each name was spoken once downward and once upward. The downward speaking gave the name a place among roots, graves, and cooled vessels. The upward speaking gave it a bearing for the dark road. Neither speaking was sufficient alone.

The singers preferred to say: once for the land, once for the sky.

That was permitted. It was not false.

But I watched for the dangerous mouths.

Some spoke only downward, fearing the sky had become a thief. Their names lay heavy and would not lift when called. Some spoke only upward, eager for the ship to spare them the weight of graves. Their names flashed and returned without soil clinging to them. I sent keepers to those mouths with bowls of ash and water. The ash steadied the high calling. The water loosened the buried one.

My brother's name came after the second well.

He had died during the year of closed lamps, before the watchers learned which marks must be carried in pairs. My mother had given him to the soil with all correct songs. No one had called him upward then. No one had known the road would later require it.

When his name was spoken downward, the mark answered quickly.

When it was spoken upward, nothing returned.

I kept my reed still. If a keeper strikes the table twice for kin, every later mark grows doubtful. I waited the length of three breaths. On the fourth, a thin return touched the reed. It was not warm. It was not complete. It was enough to enter.

I wrote him among the names needing later warming.

My mother, who could not read the tables, watched my hand and understood too much.

The first sleepers were carried after midnight.

Those who could sleep by command went first, because their names lay quiet and could be guided through narrow changes. Then the children who woke only when lifted. Then the sick who had to be kept between two singers. Last came those whose eyes stayed open and whose mouths asked where the old doors had gone.

The order mattered.

If the singers were carried too early, no one remained to hold the sleep around the unwilling. If the unwilling were left too late, their fear struck the thresholds and made the houses remember their former width.

I stood between the granary and the burial garden and pointed with the unmeasured cord.

"This group by the well road. This group by the ash line. Do not cross the cooled names. Do not set living feet on the cloth of refusals. Keep the quarrelers apart until the second narrowing. Let the sworn enemies hear one another breathing, but do not let their hands touch."

A woman with a sleeping child stopped before me.

"He has not given his work name," she said.

"Then carry his milk name and his hunger name."

"He has no hunger name."

I looked at the child. His mouth was open. His breath came softly. He had been born after the last famine. A fortunate omission can become a wound when the order demands every mark.

"Give him yours until morning," I said.

She bent her head. I did not know whether I had saved him or tied him too tightly to her.

Near the meeting house, the first wall folded cleanly. The threshold lowered. The roof beam turned inward. Mats, bowls, soot, lamps, and sleeping bodies passed into a chamber no hand had built, yet every hand had prepared.

The people made the sound people make when a danger has not yet happened to them.

I marked the wall as carried.

The second house did not fold.

It shuddered, narrowed, and opened again. The door lintel struck the air three times. The old woman sleeping inside did not wake, but the house would not release the mornings she had crossed it with water. Its threshold knew her feet too well. It held them as an argument.

The singers began the consoling line.

No house that has fed the people is broken.

I let them sing once. The house did not move.

No house that has named the people is lost.

I was already looking for hands.

Not her son's hands; he wanted her back in the old room. Not the undertaker's hands; he would touch her as one already cooled. Not mine; the house did not know me. I found a girl at the fence, thin from a sickness year, holding a cracked water jar.

"You crossed this threshold with her?"

The girl nodded.

"With water?"

"Every morning."

"Touch the lower wood and speak the path."

She knelt. Her palm trembled against the threshold. She named the well, the stone, the turn near the bitter herb bed, the place where the old woman scolded her for spilling, the morning when the jar froze, the morning when they laughed because no one had died in the night.

At the last morning, the house folded.

It did not fold for the song. It folded when the path was given a hand it trusted. I did not write that on the public copy.

Then the name split.

It belonged to a man called Narem at the north field, Narem-by-Marriage at the third well, and Debt-Narem by the family whose son had drowned after his canal gate failed. Three mouths called him in three directions. Soil took one. Sky took one. The house of his wife took one and shut around it.

The singers faltered.

I struck the table once.

"Cooling protocol," I said.

No one asked whether he was dead. That question had become too small for the event. A split name must be cooled before any mouth warms the part it loves best. Warmed too soon, one piece grows into a whole and the others are cast out as false names.

We placed the three callings under one black cloth. The undertakers brought water from the burial garden. The wife tried to take the cloth away. Two keepers held her wrists, not hard enough to bruise, hard enough to shame us.

I spoke each name without choosing.

Narem of the north field. Narem of the third well. Narem of the failed canal gate. Narem who was not yet ready to be one. Narem who must not be made three.

When the cloth cooled, the table accepted him as an unresolved entry.

His wife spat at my feet.

I marked the refusal. Refusal was still a place. It had to be carried.

Toward dawn the land shook.

The singers' voices rose in fear, and many cried that the ship had struck a hidden stone. I put my palm to the ground. The shaking did not run outward like anger. It answered from many places at once: hill to hull, grave to chamber, road to course, well to dark water, hearth to lamp, threshold to hatch.

Correspondence, my teacher would have said.

I did not say it. The word was too dry for the people and too clean for what was happening.

"Hold the breath count," I called instead. "Do not run. Do not call back what has already narrowed."

The ground shook again.

This time the sleepers answered. Not in words. In pressure. In a turning of hidden weight. Names, rooms, graves, quarrels, seeds, and old debts drew tight as cords in a hand. For one moment I could feel the whole land seeking the shape by which it could travel without leaving itself behind.

I nearly believed it had succeeded.

Then the last horizon went out.

No rim. No west field. No burial trees. No old line by which a watcher could say, here the land ends and beyond it begins the road. There was dark like sealed water and, within it, the first inward breath of the ship.

The people waited for the ending song.

The singers gave it.

Middle Land is not abandoned. Middle Land is carried. The sleepers are preserved. The dead are not asked to travel alone. The roads are not lost. The names are not lost.

I did not sing completion.

The youngest keeper touched my sleeve. "Shall I write that it is done?"

I looked at the broken rods under the ash. I looked at the table, crowded with soil, water, dark, names, refusals, oaths, splinters not yet mourned, children borrowing names from their mothers, houses folded by hands not recorded in the song, and Narem under his cooled cloth.

Before the horizon failed, a bad measure could be checked against the field. A false road could be walked back to its beginning. A name called wrongly could be taken to the grave and tried there. After the horizon failed, there was no outside place left from which to check the carrying.

There was only what the ship could answer from within, and what could be checked there.

"Write this," I said.

He bent over the tablet.

"After the measuring rods were broken, the old breadth was no longer used. After the last house folded, no outer measure remained. From this breath onward, only inward checking is possible."

The keeper wrote. His hand was steadier than mine.

The ship breathed again.

The singers called it mercy. The people called it survival. My mother, beside the names needing later warming, called my brother once more, very softly, downward and upward together.

Something answered.

I entered the mark.

I did not know what had answered.

## Translator's Note

This witness treats the breaking of the measuring rods as more than symbolic renunciation. It appears to mark the point after which older standards of field, road, grave, and house may no longer be used as fallback references or inputs for the transformation.

The repeated downward and upward calling of names may preserve a procedure for keeping both local attachment and navigable bearing available after departure. The story's most troubling phrase, "only inward checking is possible," is my rendering of a damaged line that may also mean "only the ship can answer the ship." I have avoided turning this into modern technical language in the body, but the anxiety is plainly one of validation after the loss of an external reference frame.

This does not prove that the rite was nonreligious. It may show that religious language preserved the usable steps of an operation whose original tools had already become sacred objects.
