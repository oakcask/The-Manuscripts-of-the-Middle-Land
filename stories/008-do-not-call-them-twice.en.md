# Do Not Call Them Twice

> The namekeeper wrote three marks after the first calling: manifestly recognized, recognized, not yet recognized. Beside the names most beloved, the keeper wrote a fourth command in a smaller hand: do not call them twice.

Before I chose the names to call, I made the list of names that would not be called.

This was not the part the elders wished to see. They had asked me for three names, three answers, and a clean saying for the people: the sent ones had not been lost. I gave them neither the saying nor the names at first. I sat on the cold floor before the chamber of cooled names and wrote the forbidden list while the grave keepers washed the bowls.

The first names I set aside belonged to houses whose grief was still stronger than hearing. If the answer came, they would feed on the response until it thinned. If no answer came, they would call again with sharper mouths.

The second names belonged to houses where the quarrel had not been forgiven. A brother who wanted a debt settled by the dead. A daughter who wanted the old refusal changed. A widow who had learned the exact question that would make her right. I wrote each name and drew a line beside it.

The third names belonged to elders.

I did not write that reason in the margin.

An elder can mourn with a public face and still desire proof more than comfort. I had heard them outside the chamber speaking softly of witness, of order, of a sentence the singers could carry. They did not say weapon. They did not say rule. They did not say that one true answer would make every future refusal sound like disobedience.

So I wrote their dead among the names not to be called.

My office is mistaken by those who love me. They think I keep names as a storehouse keeps grain. They bring me birth names, field names, shame names, names spoken only by one mouth at night. They ask me to protect them from loss. I do that when I can.

But a name is not kept by owning it.

A name is kept by knowing where not to place the next question.

At dawn the bowls were ready. Ash, water, soil, and grain were set before the first bright chamber. The singers stood with their hands uncovered. The grave keepers carried tablets darkened by old rain. Children were allowed to stand behind the second rope, where they could see the washing but not the tablets.

The names were called in the old order.

Birth.

Burial.

Debt.

Forgiveness.

Field.

Threshold.

Quarrel.

Song.

Kindness.

I feared the last order most.

No house calls back the dead for a field boundary so early in the night. No mother spends her first desperate need asking about a roof beam. But kindness leaves small hooks in the mouth. The loaf given in a hard winter. The hand placed over a cup before anger could spill. The laugh forgiven in a room of mourning. A name called by kindness comes close enough to be begged.

The singers knew this. That is why they put kindness last.

Or that is why I feared them.

The dead and the sleepers were washed with the same water.

I watched the name tablets, not the faces. The grave keeper touched each brow from temple to temple. A dead man whose jaw had been bound. A sleeping woman whose fingers moved when her son breathed too near. A child who had not died and had not woken for three summers. A body already light enough that the cloth over it trembled when no air moved.

The families watched faces. That was their right.

I watched the tablets because my danger was different. If I looked too long at the face, I would remember the wish around the name and not the name. I would call someone's loneliness. I would call someone's unfinished apology. I would call the child the mother needed, not the child who had been sent.

A boy behind the rope asked why the same water touched the dead and the living.

One of the singers answered that all who traveled needed a clean brow.

That was a beautiful answer. It was also incomplete.

The same water taught the living not to trust the old border between the dead and the living. It taught the dead not to be claimed by death alone. It taught my hand that when I later called, no answer could be sorted by the washing. I hated that teaching. I also received it.

When the first chamber opened, the light did not pour out.

It listened first.

Every bowl shone without flame. The ash lifted in a gray thread. The soil darkened and held its shape. The grain clicked softly against the clay as if counted by a hand too small to see.

Then the sent ones began to loosen.

Bodies loosened from weight. Voices loosened from throats. Names loosened from the places where we had kept them for lack of better mercy. Some went cleanly. Some brightened so fiercely the singers lowered their eyes. Some cooled before the chamber had finished receiving them.

One name pulled back.

The mother reached for it, and the rope burned her palm. She did not cry out. That restraint hurt the room more than a cry would have.

The elders looked at me. They wanted a word that would make the pulling back bearable.

"Not yet loosened," I said.

I did not call it love. Love was there, but love is not a measure. Love will claim any motion that moves toward it. Love will call a hook an embrace if the pain is familiar enough.

The grave keeper placed more soil near the tablet. The singer lowered the line of song until the name stopped striking the edge of the chamber. It did not return to the body. It did not go cleanly. It stayed at the limit until the light took it without triumph.

Afterward the people did not leave.

They had watched too much to return to food. They had given too much to be told nothing could be heard. The elders came with their public faces. Behind them were the three families I had chosen because their grief was not clean, but it was bearable; because their quarrels were not absent, but they would not sharpen the first answer into a knife; because if the answer came, they might still let it go.

I had chosen a warm name first.

Mara, daughter of En, had answered by warmth all through the preparation. Bread warmed near her tablet. A brass cup clouded when her sister breathed the name. During the sending, her light had thinned without tearing. The singers believed she would answer kindly.

Kindly was not what I needed.

I needed one recognition question with an answer that could be wrong.

Her sister knelt beside the bowl and put both hands under her own knees so she would not reach forward.

"Mara," I called, "daughter of En, who was sent in ash, water, soil, and grain. If this calling reaches your kept name, answer only once."

The bowl warmed.

The sister shut her eyes.

I asked the prepared question. "What did you hide under the threshold before the second flood?"

The warmth gathered on the left side of the bowl, as it had gathered in life on the left side of Mara's sleeping mat. The answer came in no voice I could point to.

"The blue bead. Not the silver one. I gave the silver one away."

The sister bent forward until her forehead touched the floor.

It was true.

I knew it before she spoke. Truth has a smell in a room that has prepared for lies. It does not smell clean. It smells like food carried past a starving person.

The people breathed in.

That was the dangerous moment. Not the answer. The breathing in. A whole room learning the shape of a new hunger.

I marked the tablet with the first recognition sign: manifestly recognized.

The sister whispered Mara's name again.

I covered the bowl before the second syllable could warm.

The second name had been cold since burial.

Tovan of the red door had answered by precise difference, never by tenderness. His tablet did not warm when touched by kin. It did not dim when cursed by an old debtor. If another name was placed too near, the frost on Tovan's tablet sharpened. In the chamber of cooled names, he lay apart without complaint.

His sons hated this.

They had come wanting their father made warm enough to forgive them. I had almost set his name aside for that reason. But Tovan had been exact in life. He had hated being mistaken more than being disliked. It seemed a poor honor to demand sweetness from him after light-burial.

I called him.

Nothing happened for so long that the younger son began to shake.

Then the bowl cooled in one narrow line from rim to center. The grain on the clay separated: three seeds to the right, four to the left. The room waited for a voice. No voice came.

I asked, "Which door was yours before the door was painted red?"

The cold line crossed the bowl again. The three seeds moved back. The four stayed.

The elder son said, "It means the western door."

"No," said the younger. His face had changed. "It means the one with four nailheads. Before the paint. He always counted them when he came home drunk."

The frost stayed.

Not warm. Not kind. Not enough for sons who had hoped their father would come back with a softer mouth.

But not mistaken.

I marked the tablet with the second recognition sign: recognized.

The younger son asked, "Will he never speak?"

I almost answered as a singer would have answered. I almost said that some names speak by measure, that cold is also a kind of keeping, that not all mercy resembles a hand.

Instead I said, "He has answered once."

The third name was the child's.

I had argued against it until my throat hurt.

The elders said the people needed to know that no small name had been spilled in the sending. The mother said nothing. That was why I consented. Had she begged, I would have refused. Had she demanded proof, I would have put the child among those not to be called. She sat with her shawl pulled over her hands and watched the empty place before the bowl.

The child had been called Lio in the house and Iren on the birth tablet. One name from the mother, one from the father's line. The father had died before the third winter. In the last fever the child had answered to both.

During light-burial the name had clung to the tongues of other children. Three of them spoke in sleep while the chamber shone. One called Lio. One called Iren. One made only the wet sound children make before language gives them a wall.

I set both name tablets beside the bowl.

"Lio-Iren," I called, though no one had called the child that in life. "Iren-Lio. Child of the north house, child of the red shawl, child who was sent in ash, water, soil, and grain. If this calling reaches your kept name, answer only once."

The bowl did not warm.

It did not cool.

It filled with the smell of milk left near sleep.

The mother made a sound and bit it in half.

I asked the prepared question because my office required it and because I despised my office in that moment.

"Who held you when the lamps failed?"

Two answers came.

"Mother."

"No one. I was holding the dark."

They came together. Not one after another. Not quarreling. Not agreeing. The mother's cry tore through both.

The elders rose. The singers leaned toward the bowl. Someone said the father's name. Someone said this proved the child had been gathered whole. Someone else said no, it proved the child had carried fear into the light. The mother reached forward with both hands.

I struck the bowl with the flat of my palm.

The sound stopped the room.

I had not known I would do it. My hand burned from the rim to the wrist. The two name tablets lay still.

"No more questions," I said.

An elder said, "We must know which answer is true."

I looked at the mother first. If she had asked me, I do not know whether I would have had strength. But she was holding her own hands against her breast, and her mouth was closed around the child's names.

"No," I said. "If we ask again, we do not learn the child. We teach the wound the shape of our question."

The elder turned away from me. He turned to the singers, because singers can make refusal sound obedient.

They did not help him.

The people waited for the saying.

I had known this would come. The first answer had been true. The second could not be mistaken. The third had come with two mouths and one hunger. The room needed a sentence quickly, before grief invented one.

So I gave the smallest sentence I could defend.

"What was called answered."

The chief elder frowned.

"Say that they returned," he said softly.

I wiped the rim of the bowl. "I cannot."

"The people need comfort."

"Then give them comfort from your own mouth."

He did not strike me. He was not a cruel man. Cruel men are easier to refuse. He was tired and frightened and responsible for a people who had just learned that some part of the sent ones might still hear them. He wanted a gate with a keeper. I was the keeper. He wanted the gate named mercy.

I wanted the gate opened only for necessary callings.

That evening I made the record.

First I copied the names not called. I did this before writing the answers, so the forbidden list would not become an afterthought. Beside some names I wrote grief too strong. Beside some, quarrel unclosed. Beside some, witness sought. Beside the elders' dead I wrote no reason, only a longer line.

Then I wrote the three called names.

Mara, daughter of En: manifestly recognized.

Tovan of the red door: recognized.

Lio-Iren, Iren-Lio: not yet recognized.

I paused over the last mark. Not yet recognized is not refusal. It is not failure. It is the covered bowl. It is the hand stopped before it reaches again. It is the knowledge that a second question can bruise what the first one reached.

Outside, families had begun teaching one another what had happened. Already the story was sweeter in some mouths and darker in others. Already Mara had returned. Already Tovan had forgiven no one. Already the child had spoken from two worlds, or from one wound, or from the mother's longing. By morning, each house would have its own answer.

I could not prevent that.

I could only guard the list.

Before I covered the record, I returned to the names most likely to be begged for. The mother with the shawl. The sister with her forehead to the floor. The sons who had learned that precise difference is a poor substitute for embrace. The houses where kindness had left its hooks.

Beside those names, in a smaller hand, I wrote the rule that would be called mercy by some and cruelty by others.

Do not call them twice.

Then I placed my own name at the bottom of the page, not among those called and not among those forbidden.

I wrote it there so that if I ever asked to hear one of them again for my own comfort, another namekeeper would know which name to withhold.

## Translator's Note

This namekeeper witness does not deny that the first answers were meaningful. Its caution falls on the use of answers as proof, property, or repeatable consolation.

The three marks rendered here as "manifestly recognized," "recognized," and "not yet recognized" do not settle what answered. The sequence records degrees of recognition rather than proof of return. The first mark may record an answer fully joined to the name, the second an answer reliably separated from other names, and the third an answer whose recognition has not been completed or should not be trained further by questioning. None of the three requires the answer to be a returned person; none excludes it.

The rule "do not call them twice" may protect the sent names from wear or from learning the shape of repeated need, or it may protect the office of the namekeeper from questions it could not survive. Later copies often soften the final sentence into a pastoral command. This witness leaves the rule harsher than that.
