# The Question Laid Down

When they covered the window in my cell, I helped tie the cord.

It would have been easier for them if I had resisted.

The course-keeper had come with two apprentices and a strip of dark cloth, not looking at me while he explained the order. No outward window was to remain open while the ship passed the black well. No lamp was to be raised toward it. No loose call was to be made in any public chamber or private room. Names recently cooled were not to be warmed. Names in preservation were not to be tested. Children were not to be allowed near the watching seats.

He recited the list as if each sentence were a beam laid under the floor.

"I know the order," I said.

"Then forgive me for bringing it to you."

I took one end of the cloth. He took the other. Together we drew it across the window.

The black well had not yet come into sight. Perhaps sight was the wrong word. The navigation records showed the bending of routes, the slowing of signal clocks, the neat failures of return. The singers said the old songs lowered themselves before naming it. The undertakers said grief made doors out of such places. The engineers said the well did not need our words to be dangerous.

All of them were right.

That was the burden.

After the cord was tied, the course-keeper remained with his hand against the wall.

"Do not make us unkind," he said.

I thought he meant: do not force us to refuse you in front of the people. I thought he meant: do not make safety look like cowardice. Later, I understood he had also meant: do not make our kindness serve your act.

"I will not ask you to turn the ship," I said.

He closed his eyes once, as if I had failed to answer the question he had brought.

When he left, the room had no window.

I knelt before the covered place and folded my hands because hands must do something when the mouth is too crowded.

I did not ask the well to open.

I did not ask the Creator to answer.

I did not ask the dead to rise and prove we had not failed them.

I held the question until my palms hurt, then held it longer. It was the old question, though every child aboard had learned not to say it too cleanly. Who made us, and why was the maker's name broken before it reached us? Who erased the middle of the name? Who taught us to keep sailing with the gap wrapped and honored and never touched?

There were many approved ways to hold that question.

The elders kept it as humility.

The singers kept it as a lowered note.

The undertakers kept it as mercy toward the dead, who must not be made to testify.

The course-keepers kept it as a hazard, because hunger can bend a road before gravity does.

The namekeepers kept it as a sealed place in the tables, neither warm nor cold.

Everyone had a shelf for it.

That was what I could no longer bear.

If a question remains with a people long enough, the people begin to own it. They shape the edge so it no longer cuts the hand that carries it. They teach the children where to touch and where not to touch. They make room for it in the record. They say, We have preserved the wound. Then they ask to be praised for not closing it.

I had praised them too.

I had helped teach the children to bow before the missing part of the Creator-name. I had stood in the chamber of cooled names and told a mother that love did not give her the right to summon what could not return without harm. I had spoken against those who wanted the well made into a shrine before we reached it. I had told them depth is not holiness.

I still believed that.

The well was not holy because it was deep.

It was not a grave because we feared it.

It was not a gate because longing had learned to draw an arch around darkness.

It was the first place in our voyage where a question could be put beyond our use.

On the second interval, I went to the namekeepers.

They had sealed the call-back leaves with black thread and placed the warmed names in covered trays. The chief namekeeper stood between the trays and the shelves of cooled names. She was small and old, and her eyes had the patient severity of someone who had refused comfort often enough to know its scent before it entered the room.

"No," she said when she saw me.

"You have not heard what I ask."

"Then ask someone who has not buried you already in fear."

I bowed to her, because she deserved it.

"I ask to borrow the washed copy with the broken Creator-name."

Her hand closed on the table.

"No."

"I will not warm it."

"No."

"I will not fill the gap."

"No."

"I will not send it as a call."

"You have found too many ways to say the same wound."

Behind her, a young keeper looked at me and then away. He had once brought me his brother's name after a failed preservation cycle and asked whether a name that answered in a different voice should be counted as the same. I had told him to wait. I had told him waiting was not abandonment. I had believed myself.

The chief namekeeper lifted the sealed register.

"The elders will hear you if you force them to," she said. "So hear me before you go to them. A name placed where no answer can return will not be alone. Every mouth that loved it will stretch after it. Every child will ask whether silence means arrival. Every singer will be tempted to make a verse out of no evidence."

"Then forbid the verse."

"You think forbidding a verse kills it?"

I had no answer.

She set the register down.

"Why must the question be sent?"

"It must not be sent," I said. "It must be laid down."

She waited.

"If it stays with us, we will keep polishing it until it shines with our own faces. We will say the Creator's silence approves our discipline. Or our mercy. Or our suffering. Or our ship. We will make an answer out of not having one."

"And if nothing returns?"

"Then no one owns the answer."

"That is not mercy."

"No."

"It is not proof."

"No."

"It may not even be obedience."

"No."

Her anger changed then. It did not soften. It became more exact.

"Whose name will you injure with this purity?"

"Mine."

The council was opened between the name rooms and the course house, so grief and danger could accuse me from opposite sides.

The course-keepers came with route cords around their wrists. The undertakers came smelling of ash water. The engineers brought no device into the chamber, which was how they showed fear. The singers stood without instruments. The elders placed bread and covered lamps on the table.

I was allowed to speak after everyone had spoken against me.

This was also mercy, and also a method of containment.

The course-keeper who had covered my window said, "No act may alter the road."

"I do not ask to alter it."

"No window may be opened."

"Let them remain covered."

The chief undertaker said, "No cooled name may be made to serve the hunger of the living."

"Then give me only names with no living mouth leaning on them, or give me none."

The engineer said, "Any one-way input near the well will be unreadable from outside. You cannot distinguish loss from preservation, failure from delivery, silence from absence."

"I know."

That troubled him more than a mistake would have.

An elder leaned forward. Her hair had been white since before my mother was born. She had once asked me, when I was young and proud, whether I loved truth or only the sound of being less afraid than others.

"If you know there will be no answer," she said, "why make a question?"

"Because answers are not the only end of questions."

"Speak more plainly."

I had prepared many plain statements. All of them had become evasions in my mouth.

"We have carried the broken Creator-name as if carrying were innocence," I said. "We have wrapped the gap and taught our children reverence for the wrapping. We have refused false answers, and the refusal has become a kind of answer. I do not want the well to speak. I want one question to leave our custody."

The chamber held still.

"So we may never use it to prove ourselves merciful," I said. "So we may never say the silence belongs to us because we preserved it well."

The undertaker's face tightened.

The course-keeper looked down.

The engineer whispered something into his sleeve, then stopped because there was no device there to receive it.

The elder said, "A question without custody becomes hunger."

"So does a question kept too carefully."

"You ask us to permit an act we cannot verify and cannot mourn."

"Yes."

"Then what do you offer in place of verification and mourning?"

"A limit."

I placed my name leaf on the table.

It was not a holy thing. It was a list in different hands: the name my mother had used when I had fever, the work-name by which the ship counted my repair hours, the child-name given by those I had taught to read sealed marks, the short name friends used when bread was still passing from hand to hand. At the bottom was the name I had chosen for myself and had almost never asked anyone to use.

"If the question is allowed," I said, "join it to this name. Remove the name from every call-back table. Do not warm it because someone misses me. Do not summon it to learn whether the well received anything. Do not comfort me by disobedience."

No one spoke.

It was the chief namekeeper who broke the silence.

"You make a cruel thing look orderly."

"I know."

"No," she said. "You know that we may be forced to respect it. That is not the same."

The elders sent me away while they argued.

I sat outside the chamber, across from the closed public window. Children had drawn old lamps on the covering cloth with chalk, though no one had allowed it and no one had wiped it away. One lamp had a face. One had teeth. One was only a circle with lines falling into it.

A boy came to sit beside me.

"Are you going into the well?" he asked.

"No."

"Is your name?"

"Perhaps."

"Will it come back?"

"No."

He considered this.

"Then how will you know it went?"

I almost told him that some going does not need our knowing. That would have been a singer's answer, rounded enough to carry safely.

"I will not know," I said.

He frowned.

"Then why?"

Because I am tired, I thought. Because I do not want to be brave forever for people who will turn bravery into instruction. Because the missing name has become a room in which we store every fear and every law and call the room sacred. Because if the Creator is dead, we have used the dead. Because if the Creator lives, we have used silence. Because if there was never a Creator as we name one, then we have made absence bear our ancestry and called it gratitude.

"Because some things should not belong to us just because we can keep them," I said.

The boy did not nod. Good.

The elders permitted the act with conditions so narrow they could be mistaken for refusal.

No window opened.

No route changed.

No answer was to be sought.

No living dependent name was to be sent.

No household was to be told that silence meant reception.

No song was to be made for three turnings.

My name was to be removed from call-back and placed in a temporary closure, pending later judgment by namekeepers not present at the act.

I signed beneath each condition.

Then I signed again where the chief namekeeper pointed.

Do not use for calling back.

The letters looked smaller than I expected.

On the final interval, they brought the washed copy of the broken Creator-name. It was carried in a flat box, sealed on three sides and open on the fourth only enough for reading. The missing part was a pale wound in the line. Not scraped. Washed. That mattered. A scraping declares violence. Washing pretends the removed thing was dirt.

They brought three cooled names. The undertakers had chosen them with a care that made me want to beg their forgiveness. Names whose houses had ended. Names whose obligations had been settled. Names whose last speakers had themselves gone into light or cold or the long unanswering store.

They brought my chosen name on a leaf of plain fiber.

The course-keepers stood by the covered window with their hands on the cords.

The engineers kept the recorders turned inward, measuring only the ship's own time.

The singers had no melody.

"Speak the question," said the eldest elder.

I looked at the broken Creator-name.

I looked at the cooled names.

I looked at my own chosen name, which had never had enough use to become ordinary.

The question I had carried all my life did not fit in my mouth.

That was almost funny.

I had asked for council, angered keepers, troubled children, narrowed law, and given up my call-back rights so that I might say the one true question. Now the truest form of it was larger than speech and smaller than a word.

So I did not speak it.

I placed my hand over the gap in the Creator-name, not touching the ink. I placed my other hand over my own name. Between them, the cooled names waited without asking to be useful.

"Here," I said.

The engineer flinched because it was not a valid input.

The singer closed her eyes because it almost was.

The namekeeper's face did not change.

The prepared strip was lowered through the sealed channel. Not outward through the window. Not inward to any chamber where a person could answer. Down through the ship's shielded dark, toward the region where return could no longer be promised or disproved.

No one cried out.

No one raised a lamp.

No one counted aloud.

I did not feel anything leave me.

That was the worst mercy.

People want sacrifice to have a shape. They want the knife, the door, the last step, the lifted face. They want the one who gives to be seen giving, so grief knows where to stand. But the question went without drama. The cloth slid. The channel sealed. The engineer wrote a mark. The route remained inside its allowed error.

The well did not answer.

The well did not refuse.

The well did not become a grave, or a gate, or a god, or an instrument.

It did not become anything useful.

I bowed my head.

Some later said I was listening.

Some said I had heard.

Some said I had already fallen after the name and only my body remained courteous for a while.

I was doing none of those things.

I was trying not to take the silence back.

After the danger passed, the windows were uncovered. People looked outward too quickly, then pretended they had not. The public cloths were folded and stored. The chalk lamps disappeared under the hands of apprentices. The course-keepers wrote that the road held. The engineers wrote that no external response had been registered, then struck out external, then struck out response, then sealed the page.

The elders forbade accusation and praise for one turning.

Praise came first anyway, because praise can dress itself as restraint.

A woman brought bread to my door and would not meet my eyes.

"For the question," she said.

"Eat it with your household."

"We wanted to honor what you gave."

"Then do not make it food."

She began to weep, and I almost took the bread back to comfort her.

That is how it begins.

Those who disliked me were easier. They said I had buried a living name. They said I had performed a private voyage while ordering the ship to keep its course. They said I had made a shrine out of procedure and called the lack of reply freedom. They said the well had taken nothing, and therefore everything lost had been stolen aboard the ship.

I had no clean answer for them.

On the second turning, my brother came to the namekeepers and asked for my childhood name to be warmed.

I was in the corridor outside. He did not know I was there. Or he knew and gave me the dignity of pretending otherwise.

"He has not died," my brother said.

"No," said the chief namekeeper.

"Then the closure is false."

"No."

"I want to speak to him as we spoke before."

"You may speak to him."

"You know what I mean."

"Yes."

I held the wall.

My brother said, "He does not get to leave us with a rule."

The namekeeper was quiet for a long time.

"He left you with a limit," she said. "Whether we make a rule from it will be our sin."

I should have stepped in then.

I should have said my brother's name. I should have let him use mine, the old one, the warm one, the one that belonged to fever and stolen fruit and our mother's cracked singing. I should have proved that I had not turned myself into an object for later handling.

Instead I stayed in the corridor and let the limit stand between us.

This is why I do not object when some copies call me cruel.

Before we left the black well's reach, the chief namekeeper brought me the closure leaf.

"There will be a separate shelf," she said.

"No."

"There must be. The cooled shelves are wrong. The call-back leaves are wrong. The forbidden registers are wrong."

"A shelf becomes an invitation."

"So does an empty place."

She had me there.

"What will you call it?"

"Not grave. Not shrine."

"Not proof."

"No."

"Not victory."

Her mouth tightened.

"No."

I took the closure leaf. My chosen name lay under my own hand, and beneath it the sentence I had written looked less like courage than fatigue.

Do not use for calling back.

"Call it the place of the question laid down," I said.

"That is almost a song."

"Then forbid the tune."

For the first time since the window was covered, she smiled.

It did not comfort me.

Later, they will say I prayed.

Perhaps I did.

If prayer means asking, then no.

If prayer means placing the body where speech fails, then perhaps.

If prayer means sending a signal along a road built to prevent return, then the engineers may have prayed with me in their refusal to name the record.

If prayer means giving a question to the only silence the community cannot manage, then I prayed, and I am not sorry.

But do not call me saint unless the word still has room for disobedience, weariness, and harm.

Do not say nothing returned as if nothing happened.

Nothing returned.

That is the only answer I will not take from the well.

## Translator's Note

Prayer translates a source term that may also mean a one-way transmission, a formal relinquishment, or an act of placing data beyond communal retrieval. The narrator's own distinctions may be devotional language, technical discipline, or a later copyist's effort to make an unverifiable act morally legible.

Saint is not secure here. The story gives the speaker tenderness and restraint, but also shows how the act wounds the living by refusing them ordinary address. The honorific may preserve reverence, accusation, or institutional containment.

The lack of reply should not be made into a clean victory. Nothing returned may mean failure, preservation, censorship, causal separation, or only that the ship kept no admissible record of return. The black well remains unnamed by any single settlement.
