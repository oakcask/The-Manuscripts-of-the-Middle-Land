# The Word Beside the Name

> They placed the refusal beside the name, and the name beside the bowl. Whether the refusal was kept, softened, or carried away, no singer agreed to say.

When I heard that a refusal could be placed beside a name, I knew the rite had learned a new hunger.

The elder spoke of it as mercy. He stood at the door of the cooled chamber with his hands washed and empty, and he said no one would be sent without the word that had stood near them in life. Consent would be placed there. Fear would be placed there. A quarrel, if the quarrel had not been healed. A debt. A forgiveness. A last kindness remembered by a neighbor when the family could not bear to remember anything.

"And refusal?" I asked.

The elder looked at me as if I had brought him a wounded thing.

"Refusal also," he said. "Especially refusal. We will not make the dead agreeable by leaving their no behind."

The people around him lowered their eyes in relief. Some had feared the rite would smooth every mouth. Some had feared their beloved would be carried into light with a false peace on the tongue. The elder had answered them well.

That was the trouble.

If he had said the no would be scraped away, I could have stood against him cleanly. If he had ordered silence, I could have shown the children where silence had been ordered. But he promised to keep the refusal. He promised to place it by the name, near the bowl, near the ash, near the water, near the soil, near the grain.

He promised that nothing would be lost.

I had already learned to fear that promise.

The preparation began before the lamps were trimmed.

The grave keepers brought ash from the houses where death had been named aloud. The seed stewards brought grain from jars that had not been opened since the first folding of the fields. Children came with soil in their hands, because the singers said a handful given by a child reminded the earth to be gentle. Water came last, covered in blue cloth.

I watched the bowls set out in a line.

Ash, for what had burned and remained.

Water, for what had touched both faces and left neither clean of the other.

Soil, for the first holding.

Grain, for the later hunger.

The hands that arranged them were careful. No one laughed. No one hurried. A young keeper brushed a husk from the rim of a bowl with the tenderness a mother gives to sleep at the corner of a child's eye.

I hated him for that tenderness, and then hated myself because the hate could not hold.

It is easy to resist a cruel hand. A cruel hand gives the body a wall to press against. A careful hand makes the wall out of those you love.

My sister Leme lay among the sleepers.

She had not died. That was the first thing our mother said to anyone who looked too long at the pallet. Leme had gone into preserving sleep after the fever took her legs and left the rest of her listening. Some mornings her eyelids moved when our mother ground barley. Some evenings her mouth shaped the first sound of my name and then forgot the road outward.

Now she was carried with the dead.

Not in the same row. The singers were too wise for that. They placed the newly dead at the east wall and the sleepers at the west, with a path between them wide enough for grief to believe in difference.

Then the grave keeper uncovered the water.

He washed the forehead of old Tovan first. Tovan was dead, and had been dead long enough that his daughter's crying had become work. The keeper's thumb crossed the brow from temple to temple. The water darkened and shone.

Then he turned to Leme.

"No," I said.

My mother caught my sleeve. "Do not shame her."

"She is not dead."

"No one has said she is."

That was the second trouble. They were careful with the words. No one said the sleepers were dead. No one said the dead were only sleeping. The same water did what the mouths refused to do.

The keeper bent over Leme. His thumb moved over her brow in the same path.

Her eyelids trembled.

For one breath I thought she objected. Then I saw that the water was cold.

A child near the path asked, "Why does the same water touch them both?"

His father reached for his shoulder, but the singer beside them knelt before the hand could close.

"Because all who are carried must be known by the same road," she said.

It was beautiful. The child nodded, not satisfied but quieted. The sharp part of the question entered the song and came back rounded, safe to hold. I wanted to take the child by both shoulders and say, Ask again. Ask until the answer has teeth.

But the singer had not lied.

That was how the rite kept winning.

Names were called after the washing.

Birth, burial, debt, forgiveness, field, threshold, quarrel, song, kindness. Each name was touched by the words that had held it in the village. A dead woman's debt was named by the man she had cheated and later fed through winter. A sleeper's kindness was named by a girl who had once been given a torn sandal. My sister's name came with field, song, and the small quarrel she and I had not finished about whether our father's knife belonged to the house or the hand that used it.

Then came a name that withdrew.

It belonged to a man called Serat, who had refused the first gathering and later asked to be placed among the dead without song. When the keeper reached his bowl, the flame inside the lamp narrowed. The water tightened. The slip bearing his name curled away from the ash as if the letters had remembered a door.

I stood up.

"You see?" I said. "He is saying no."

The keeper did not look angry. He did not look afraid. He reached for a smaller weight, set it beside the slip, and spoke to the scribe.

"Mark withdrawal before loosening," he said. "Keep the name covered until second measure."

The scribe wrote it.

Withdrawal before loosening.

Covered until second measure.

The no entered the record as a step.

I wanted him to shout. I wanted the chamber to crack. I wanted Serat's name to become a stone no hand could lift. Instead the keeper made room for the refusal, and by making room he took away the place from which refusal could stand outside the rite.

Light opened in the first chamber.

It did not blaze. It gathered, pale at the rims of the bowls, then warmer where the ash had been stirred. The sleepers breathed shallowly. The dead gave no breath but changed in their stillness, becoming less held by the cloth and more held by the names. Leme's fingers loosened. Tovan's daughter covered her mouth. The child who had asked about the water leaned forward until the singer drew him back with one gentle hand.

Serat's covered bowl rang once.

No one called it testimony.

The keeper noted the sound.

When the first sending ended, the people did not move. We had been told there would be no calling back for comfort. We had been told the rite was not a door to be opened whenever grief grew hungry. Even I had almost believed that rule. A people who could call and did not call might still know that not everything could be owned.

Then the elders chose three names for proof.

They did not call it proof. They called it first hearing.

The warm name answered first. It belonged to a woman whose sons had fought over her loom. The elder asked where she had hidden the blue shuttle. The answer came from the light by the bowl, soft and irritated, as she had been in life.

"Under the cracked step, because fools look in chests."

Her youngest son fell to the floor laughing and weeping.

They found the shuttle before morning.

The answer was true.

I wish it had not been.

A false answer would have given me a clean hatred. A pretty echo could have been cursed. But truth entered the room and took the side of the rite. The sons embraced. The old quarrel loosened. The elder looked broken by relief. Even my mother, whose daughter lay among the sleepers, pressed both hands to her mouth as if warmth itself had returned to the world.

I stood there and felt the institution grow harder under my feet.

The cold name answered next, not with warmth but with difference. It would not say a hidden thing. It would not comfort the widow. Yet when two names were placed before it, it turned from the wrong one and held to its own. The keeper seemed relieved. The widow looked emptied by the smallness of it.

The child's name answered last in two voices.

The mother cried out. The father said, "Again." The singer said his name so softly that it almost became a lullaby.

"No," said the keeper.

It was the first word from him that I trusted.

The elders withdrew to make the rule. No calling for hunger. No calling to settle inheritance. No calling to make the dead bless the living. No second calling where the first answer had already wounded the name.

I hated them less while they wrote it.

That frightened me too.

Near dawn, the elder came to me with a blank slip.

"Your sister's name has been placed," he said. "Your mother's word has been placed. Leme's last spoken sound has been placed. There is room for yours."

"No."

"Then I will write that you refused."

"You will carry even that."

He did not deny it.

Behind him, my mother sat beside Leme's empty pallet. Not empty. That is too simple. The body was there, but less wholly there than the bowl, the water mark, the covered light that had taken her name far enough away that I could no longer protect it by standing near it.

My mother had not asked for the calling of Leme. She had obeyed the rule. Her restraint accused me more than pleading would have.

"If I give you my refusal," I said, "you will say I was honored."

"I will say your word stood beside her name."

"And if I give nothing?"

"Then nothing will stand there from you."

It was the first cruel thing he had said, and because it was true, I almost thanked him.

I thought of Leme waking, if waking was still a word that could reach her. I thought of her hearing every voice but mine arranged around her like bowls. I thought of my no preserved so carefully that later singers could touch it and say, See, even the resister was carried with tenderness. I thought of the warm answer under the cracked step. I thought of the child's two voices. I thought of Serat's covered bowl ringing once, then becoming a note in the hand of a patient keeper.

I would not write the full refusal.

I would not leave the place empty.

I took the reed.

On the slip, beside the bowl, beside the name that had once turned toward me in our mother's doorway, I wrote one word.

Near.

The elder read it and did not ask what it meant.

That was his mercy, or his discipline.

The slip was placed beside Leme's bowl before the light thinned. I watched it lie there, too small to stop anything, too stubborn to be blessing, too tender to be cleanly used as protest.

Near.

If she was called, perhaps it would guide her. If she was kept, perhaps it would trouble the keeping. If she was gone, perhaps it would be only a word laid where my hand could not go.

The singers later said I had softened.

The heretical copy removes the sentence.

I do not know which hand was kinder.

## Translator's Note

This resister witness shares the event core of the light-burial traditions but shifts the danger: the rite is frightening not because it ignores refusal, but because it preserves refusal so carefully that refusal may become part of the rite's own evidence.

The withdrawing name of Serat is treated by the keeper as a procedural condition rather than a revelation. Later orthodox copies read that handling as patience. The heretical line reads it as absorption: even a name's retreat becomes a mark, a measure, and a covered bowl.

The final word, "Near," is intentionally unstable. It may mean consent to remain near the sleeper, protest against removal, fear that the speaker might need to call or be called, or love reduced to the smallest mark that could still be placed beside a name.
