# Reproduction Not Permitted

> The record says that no answer returned.
> It does not say whether the record was permitted to recognize an answer.

I was assigned to the well because I did not use the word prayer.

That was the reason given on the work leaf. My hands were steady around unreturned signals. I had kept the listening frames clean through three passages of dead lamps and one passage of a cracked moon. I had written failure where the apparatus failed and silence where silence was the only honest measurement.

Also, I did not pray aloud.

The elders liked that better than they admitted.

When the black well entered the forward tables, the navigators closed the outer windows before anyone was allowed to gather. They called it synchronization protection. The undertakers called it mercy. The namekeepers called it restraint. I wrote all three phrases on separate tabs and placed them under the same clamp.

The recorder did not care which word was holier.

It only cared that the ship had begun to treat a region of the dark as if looking at it might change the ship.

That was not superstition. It was also not exactly physics.

By the second interval I had replaced the ordinary return columns with blank glass. The usual headings were too confident.

Sent.

Received.

Changed.

Lost.

Recovered.

No honest instrument could promise those words near the well. I scraped them from the frame and wrote a narrower heading with a dull stylus.

Input observed at outer boundary.

The apprentice beside me frowned.

"That is longer than the column," she said.

"Then the column was built for easier events."

She was young enough to smile. I envied her that and made her check the timing cords again.

The one later called the saint came while we were sealing the delay chamber.

He did not arrive with singers. He did not wear ash. He carried a wrapped packet against his chest with both hands, as if the packet were warm or fragile or guilty. Behind him stood one namekeeper and one undertaker, each careful not to look as if they had brought him.

"I was told the record must be prepared here," he said.

"The record is already prepared."

"For the ship?"

"For the boundary."

He looked at the blank glass where the return columns had been. "Then it is not prepared for the ship."

I disliked him at once. Not because he sounded wrong, but because he had found the weakness in the apparatus without touching it.

The namekeeper unwrapped the packet.

Inside were three kinds of leaves.

The first held a damaged Creator-name, older than any active registry and washed so many times that the missing part looked deliberate. The second held a small bundle of cooled names. Their families had ended or been folded into other houses long enough ago that no one could come to the chamber and claim a fresh wound. The third was a narrow leaf bearing the saint's own names, still warm in every ordinary sense.

I read them without speaking.

The damaged Creator-name made the instruments flutter. Not because of sanctity. Ink composition, old salts, handling heat, any of these could move a needle. I told myself that and adjusted nothing.

The cooled names lay flat.

The living name troubled me most.

"This one cannot be entered as material," I said.

The saint answered, "Then enter it as a condition."

"A living name is not a condition."

"It is when the living person removes the right of recall."

The undertaker closed his eyes. The namekeeper watched my hand, waiting to see which column I would choose.

There was no column.

That was why they had come to me.

At council, the navigators argued against any input. They did not say the well was empty. They said the ship could not afford an unscheduled act near a boundary that made schedules into guesses.

The undertakers argued against the names. They did not say nothing was kept beyond the well. They said, if anything was kept there, it must not be turned into evidence for the living.

The saint argued least.

That made him dangerous in a way the loud are rarely dangerous.

"No window need open," he said. "No course need bend. No dead one need answer. The question will not be called back."

"Then it is not a question," said the chief navigator.

The saint bowed. "Perhaps your word for question is too dependent on reply."

Everyone looked at me then, because I was the person with instruments and therefore could be mistaken for a judge.

I said, "If the act is performed, the record cannot classify it as communication."

The navigators nodded too quickly.

"Nor as failure of communication," I added.

They stopped nodding.

The eldest undertaker asked, "Can you classify it as burial?"

"No."

He seemed relieved, then wounded by his relief.

"Can you classify it as experiment?" the eldest elder asked.

I looked at the saint's wrapped names.

I had been trained to love experiment. A clean experiment was a mercy. It told the hand what it had done and what it had not done. It separated instrument from wish, witness from wound, procedure from story.

Near the black well, those separations had already begun to fray.

"Only if we pretend the input can be repeated," I said.

The council sat very still.

After that, they let me write the conditions.

The windows would remain sealed.

The ship would not alter course.

No listening frame would be tuned for a reply.

No cooled name would be used if a living household still leaned on it.

The saint's living names would be marked unavailable for recall before the act began.

The apparatus would preserve boundary timing, signal strength, local drift, and the exact absence of any confirmed return.

The act would not be repeated.

I wrote the last line three times because the stylus kept catching.

Reproduction not permitted.

Not "impossible." I would not give the council the comfort of necessity.

Not "forbidden." I would not pretend the ship had mastered the boundary by issuing an order.

Not "unholy." That was not my office.

Not "holy." That was also not my office.

Reproduction not permitted.

The saint watched me write it.

"That is a severe mercy," he said.

"It is a procedural warning."

"Yes," he said. "Those often survive longer."

I should have refused then.

Instead I went back to the chamber and prepared the instruments.

We used no open window. The boundary frame received only what the ship was already shedding: clock scatter, hull tremor, the low waste heat of a living vessel passing through a region that did not answer in the usual ways. The input channel was a legal fiction built from permitted leakages.

Most of our work was like that.

The apprentice polished the glass until her breath vanished from it.

"Do you think there is anything there?" she asked.

"There is enough there to bend our words."

"That is not what I meant."

"It is what I can record."

She looked toward the sealed cabinet where the names waited. "My mother says the Creator hid the first names in places that could not be taken by fire."

"Your mother should not say that near a live frame."

"She says it at home."

"Then keep it there."

She returned to the timing cords, ashamed. I had spoken too sharply. Her mother had not invented the hunger. None of us had. We only built better shelves for it.

At the fourth interval, the saint came to sign away recall.

The namekeeper read each of his names aloud. The ship-count name. The household name. The child-name his mother had used. The shortened name used over bread. Each one sounded ordinary enough to hurt.

The saint signed beneath them with no flourish.

Do not use for calling back.

Do not comfort me by correction.

I halted the stylus over the copy line.

"Correction?" I asked.

"If later hands decide I meant something kinder."

The namekeeper looked offended. The undertaker looked as if he wished offense were enough to stop the hand.

I copied the sentence exactly.

That was the first time I helped him.

The input began without ceremony because the council had forbidden ceremony, and because everyone present knew that forbidding ceremony can make every motion ceremonial.

The damaged Creator-name entered first.

Nothing moved in the chamber except the instruments.

On the old leaf, the washed gap crossed the frame as a measurable absence: less ink, different fiber strain, a cooler patch where many fingers had avoided touching the missing place. The recorder caught these things faithfully. It also caught the tremor of my hand when the gap passed the boundary mark.

I erased that tremor from the working copy.

Then I restored it.

Instrument disturbance caused by operator contact.

The sentence was true. It was also a kind of confession that would be unreadable to anyone who wanted certainty.

The cooled names followed.

They entered as small changes in weight and heat, as old adhesive, as breath caught and not released by the undertaker beside me. No voice rose. No lamp altered. The apprentice whispered one number twice, then corrected herself. I marked the correction and did not mark the whisper.

Last came the saint's living name.

He did not step into the frame. No body was lowered. No blood was taken. No one opened a door in the floor.

Only the leaf crossed the boundary mark.

That is the clean account.

The unclean account is that the room changed when the leaf crossed. The sealed windows seemed less sealed because every person imagined what would be seen if they were uncovered. The navigators gripped their cords as if holding the ship together by hand. The undertaker bowed his head in anger or respect. The apprentice stopped breathing until I touched her wrist.

The saint stood still.

If he prayed, he did it without moving his mouth.

The recorder wrote a line of light across the blank glass.

Not a signal. Not a reply. Not a failure. A line produced by timing delay, frame stress, and the fact that every instrument wants to complete a pattern when given an edge.

I have written that explanation many times.

I have never made it explain the line.

The line held for seven counts and then thinned until the glass looked blank again.

No answer returned.

I did not write it.

The council sent for the record before the chamber cooled.

They wanted a sentence.

The navigators wanted, "No additional drift."

The undertakers wanted, "No recall attempted."

The namekeepers wanted, "Closure mark unbroken."

The elders wanted a form that would prevent hunger without accusing the saint.

The saint wanted nothing from me. That was his last cruelty.

I stood with the working copy in my hands and understood that any single record would steal from the others. If I wrote experiment, the cooled names became material. If I wrote burial, the boundary became a grave. If I wrote censorship, the saint became a smuggler and the ship became his enemy. If I wrote preservation, grief would learn a new way to ask for storage.

"Technician," the eldest elder said, "what occurred?"

I read from the copy.

"At the marked interval, the prepared inputs crossed the permitted boundary under sealed-window conditions. No reply channel was opened. No external return was confirmed. Local records are insufficient to classify loss, retention, transmission, refusal, or reception. Reproduction not permitted by communal procedure."

The council disliked every word.

That was how I knew the record might survive.

The navigator said, "You have not said it failed."

"I cannot verify failure."

The undertaker said, "You have not said the names were buried."

"I cannot claim burial."

The eldest elder said, "You have not said the saint obeyed."

I looked at the saint then.

He looked tired, not radiant. His hands were empty. He had given the ship a wound shaped like a rule.

"I can say the procedure was not repeated," I said.

The elder accepted that because it sounded like control.

Afterward, I sealed the original twice.

The first seal placed it in the navigation archive, under boundary hazards. The second placed a duplicate in the funerary archive, under unreturned names. Neither archive would admit it belonged there. That was the point. A record kept in only one house becomes the proof that house requires.

The apprentice watched me press the second seal.

"Will later readers understand why there are two copies?"

"No."

"Then why do it?"

"So neither copy can pretend to be the whole."

She considered that and said, "That sounds like a prayer."

I told her to clean the stylus.

Years later, when the copied songs began to circulate, I saw what had been made from my line.

One copy said, The saint prayed, and the well kept the prayer.

Another said, The saint prayed, and prayer was forbidden thereafter.

A stricter copy said, The technician proved that nothing returned.

A softer one said, The technician protected the holy silence from measurement.

All of them were wrong in ways that allowed them to be sung.

The original, if it remains, does not sing.

It says the boundary was crossed by prepared inputs.

It says no reply channel was opened.

It says no external return was confirmed.

It says the local records are insufficient.

It says reproduction is not permitted.

It does not say whether we performed an experiment, conducted a burial, broke a censorship order, or placed a name where the ship could no longer turn it into comfort.

I wrote it that way because I did not believe in prayer.

Or because I understood, too late, why prayer had to be made unreadable to survive.

## Translator's Note

The technical vocabulary in this witness may be an attempt to erase religious language from the event. It may also be the opposite: a shield against later worship, preserving the act in terms too narrow and awkward to become a clean rite.

The key phrase rendered here as "Reproduction not permitted" is not simply a laboratory warning. It also touches communal procedure, funerary restraint, and a ban on turning one unreturned act into a repeatable consolation.
