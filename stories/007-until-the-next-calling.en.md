# Until the Next Calling

> The grave keeper washed the living with the water of the cooled dead. He called first into the soil, then into the thinning sky. He entered neither answer nor refusal, but only the deferral: kept until the next calling.

I began at the burial garden, because the dead should not hear last what the living have already feared.

Before the lamps were trimmed, I stood between the rows and read the names in the order of their resting. The garden held the old record: name, soil, and the weight of answering kept together. Those who answered by stone were called. Those who answered by grass were called. Those whose markers had sunk were called by the small hollows where rain gathered. Those whose kin had forgotten the second name were called by the first, and I did not rebuke the kin.

I opened no separate register for names that returned and names that did not.

That was my charge. On the night when Middle Land was to be made ready for its own carrying, no dead person was to be lost by haste, and no silent grave was to be counted as absent because a living mouth had grown afraid. The living would enter the same order by forehead and water, but order is not consent, and water is not mercy by itself.

There were three bowls at my feet. One held water drawn before the wells began to shiver. One held water that had washed the brows of the newly dead. One held the water kept with cooled names, covered with a cloth so no child would look into it and ask whose face had gone thin there.

I mixed them with my right hand. I did not bless them. Blessing belonged to the singers. I measured the chill with my wrist, as I had been taught by the old grave keeper who could tell from water alone whether a name would answer before morning.

The first living forehead was a boy's.

His mother held him from behind. His eyes were open and angry. He was old enough to know that my thumb moved over him in the path used for a body that no longer argued.

"I am not dead," he said.

"No," I said.

I washed him from temple to temple. I washed the place above the nose where a fever first gathers. I washed the hairline where mothers find dust at dusk. His mother looked away. Many did. It is hard for the living to watch their own beloved handled with the fairness of burial.

I did not say that the rite was gentle.

Gentleness was not the question placed in my hands. A hand may be gentle and still remove. A hand may tremble and still seal. I had washed infants whose names had never warmed the house. I had washed elders whose death had been expected for so long that grief arrived tired. I knew the hand's small mercy. I also knew what a hand can carry away.

After the boy came a woman who had buried two husbands and refused to lie beside either grave. After her came a man whose brother would not speak his name. After him came a sleeping girl, carried by four singers because her legs had stiffened before the song. I washed them in the same order as the dead.

Some families thanked me. Some turned their faces toward the fields. Some watched the bowl as if the water would return what it touched.

When the last forehead shone, I took the bowl to the first grave.

Each name was to be spoken twice.

Once toward the soil.

Once toward the thinning sky.

The singers taught the people to hear hope in the second calling. I do not say they were wrong. A sky may fail and still be asked. A road may close and still receive a foot. But to my ear the second calling sounded like lifting a body from a grave before the grave had forgiven the hand.

I knelt and called Mara, daughter of En, into the soil.

The soil answered slowly, but it answered. A little cold rose through my knees. The stone darkened where the name had been cut. That was enough. I called her again toward the sky, and the air took the name without weight.

I marked nothing.

I called Tovan of the red door. I called Leth who had drowned in the irrigation trench. I called the unnamed infant of the north house, whose mother had refused all naming because the child had not opened its eyes. For the infant I placed both hands on the soil, because a name refused in grief may still need room.

The soil gave back less each time.

Not nothing. Nothing would have been simpler. Less.

By the ninth row, the ground beneath the graves had begun to loosen its hold. The dead were not leaving. I will not write that. They were being made ready for a room that had not yet opened, and a ready room is not an answering mouth.

At the grave of Saran the potter, the earth would not yield.

The first call went down and came back with a taste of iron. The second call would not rise from my mouth. My tongue held it behind the teeth. Saran had fired the urns for half the burial garden. He knew the weight of ash. Perhaps the grave knew him too well to lend him to any other chamber.

The rim watchers looked toward me. The singers lowered their voices. Saran's daughter stood with both hands inside her sleeves and did not ask me to force the name.

I lay down over the grave.

My cheek took soil. My chest pressed the place where his chest had been lowered. I asked forgiveness downward first.

"If we have come too soon, hold him. If we have come too late, release him. If there is no hour that is not wrong, remember that our hands are small."

Then I asked forgiveness upward.

"If we carry what should have rested, do not let us call theft by a clean name. If we seal what should have answered, do not let us mistake silence for peace."

The grave moved under me.

Not enough to open. Enough to be heard.

I did not force the second calling. I entered Saran in the deferral, among those to be kept until the next calling. His daughter bent once, not in thanks and not in refusal. I accepted that.

Near midnight the chamber for cooled names was prepared.

It was not above the ground, and it was not beneath the ground. The ship had begun to make depth where depth had not been. The attendants with bowls and tablets walked to the burial garden's eastern edge and descended without lowering their feet.

I followed with the covered water.

Inside, the air had no season. Names already lay there in lines, each wrapped in the measure by which it had last answered: a grain's warmth, a thumb's chill, a child's wrong speaking, a widow's breath before she stopped herself. The chamber did not mourn. That was why it frightened me.

We brought in the names that could no longer be warmed without tearing.

Yaret came there in three pieces, carried by three mouths that should have waited. I heard the singer outside make him cold before love chose one shard and called it whole. I set the three pieces beside each other and laid my palm over them until the frost gathered.

Mara came later, light as thread. The unnamed infant did not come. Saran did not come.

I wrote nothing beside Saran's place.

A cooled name is not a lost name. This is the rule. A cooled name is kept from rot, from quarrel, from eager correction, from the warm falsehood that says, I know you entirely. Yet cold also takes away the mouth's first answer. A name that once rose when bread was broken may now wait through a feast without stirring.

We do this so the dead may be found.

We do this because we have already failed to find them.

When the first sleeper cried out, I was still in the chamber.

It was not the voice of the dead.

For that I was grateful, and the gratitude shamed me.

The dead do not cry with such heat. They answer late, or in weight, or through a stone darkening after rain. This cry had breath in it. It struck the chamber wall and came back red. Someone living had felt the ship take hold of a road beneath the skin.

I carried the covered bowl out.

Houses were narrowing. Paths were lifting. The burial fence had bent inward, not broken, and each grave held its shape while becoming less certain of where shape must remain. The singers called this gathering. The watchers called this readiness. I had no permitted word.

Another sleeper cried out. Then another.

I listened for the dead beneath them. I feared the cries would cover the slower answer. I feared the living, in their terror, would be heard first, and the dead would be left behind because they no longer knew how to insist.

So I returned to the rows and began again.

Mara, daughter of En.

Tovan of the red door.

Leth of the trench.

Infant of the north house, unnamed but not unheld.

Saran the potter, kept until the next calling.

I called while the land shook. I called while the old measuring rods broke under the common fire. I called while a child screamed for a game drawn in dust. I called while the sky thinned beyond prayer's former reach. I called each name first into the soil, and then, when my mouth would bear it, toward the sky.

The ship breathed before dawn.

The breath did not come from one place. It entered the graves from below and the houses from within and the sleepers through their washed foreheads. Grave records and the preservation of the living touched in the same dark, not as comfort, but as a larger charge. I felt hands that had washed my hands when I was little. I felt hands I had washed after death. I felt hands not yet born, already owed clean water.

For a moment I understood why the singers used beautiful words.

Then the moment passed.

Beauty is a lamp. It is not a witness.

When the last house folded, the old horizon went out. The meeting place entered the deep body of the ship. The burial garden no longer lay behind it or beside it. It had become a set of rooms whose doors were not yet named.

The people waited for the official ending.

I did not give it. It was not mine.

I called the dead first.

Mara, daughter of En.

Tovan of the red door.

Leth of the trench.

Infant of the north house.

Saran the potter.

Yaret in his threefold cold.

All those whose stones had answered. All those whose grass had answered. All those whose hollows had held rain. All those whose kin had forgotten and were not rebuked. All those removed from soil. All those sealed from warmth. All those carried without being asked again.

There was no return.

There was also not complete silence.

Something in the rooms held its breath after my voice. Something in the cooled chamber kept its frost without cracking. Something under Saran's unmarked place did not open and did not withdraw.

I made the only record of deferral I could make.

Kept until the next calling.

I have written that phrase many times. It does not declare rescue. It does not declare loss. It gives no comfort to those who want the dead to answer warmly from the ship's rooms. It gives no weapon to those who would say the dead were abandoned in the old soil.

It is a grave keeper's phrase.

It leaves the bowl covered.

It leaves the hand unwashed.

It leaves the name where I can reach it when I am called to call again.

## Translator's Note

This funerary witness treats ship-making less as rescue than as an enlargement of burial duty. The washing of living foreheads follows the handling of the dead, and the narrator is careful not to turn that resemblance into consolation.

"Kept until the next calling" may refer to a body, a name, a grave, or a community whose answer has been deferred. Some copies substitute "sealed" for "kept" in two places; I have not followed them, but the variant darkens the rite.

The resisted grave of Saran is not resolved. In this witness, refusal is neither overcome nor honored cleanly. It is placed under custody, which may be mercy, theft, or merely the only record left to the grave keeper.
