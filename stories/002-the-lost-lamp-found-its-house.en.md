# The Lost Lamp Found Its House

> Yet when one child asked whether the lost lamp had found its house, every watcher turned toward the child at once, and the room knew that the knowledge had not slept.

The lamp song began with the warm names.

Seed.

Hearth.

Salt.

Door.

Mother.

Blood.

Morning.

Soil.

After those came the lamp names. After the lamp names came the little turn in the song where we lifted our hands and showed the singers we still had ten fingers, two eyes, one mouth, and one house to return to.

That was how children learned the sky. First the things near enough to touch. Then the things too far to warm.

On the night before the watchers came down, my mother sang the wrong word.

She sang, "the lamps are carried."

Then she stopped.

She put her thumb against my mouth, though I had not spoken. She began again from seed. This time she sang, "the lamps return." Her voice was soft and did not fit the room.

In the morning my father said all children were to stay near the hearths. That meant we went to the doorways. Children know the length of near.

The watchers came from the high place with dust on their knees. They had washed their hands in the rim stream, but the dust had stayed in the folds. One watcher looked at the wall the way a sick person looks at water. One carried a cord under his arm. One carried a board wrapped in cloth. One had no tears on his face, but his mouth looked as if tears had passed through it.

The elders did not shout.

That frightened me.

When a child breaks a bowl, there is shouting. When a goat enters the seed store, there is shouting. When lightning strikes a roof, everyone shouts even while carrying water.

The elders spoke quietly and brought bread first.

Then they brought warm water, ash, salt, and sleeping cloth.

"Let sight rest," they said.

I knew the sleeping cloth. It was used when thunder walked over the roof and a child could not stop shaking. It smelled of smoke, milk, and old skin. The watchers were grown, but the elders wrapped them like little ones. They washed the watchers' eyelids. They touched salt to the watchers' tongues. They told them not to climb inside their own seeing while sleep held the door.

The watchers slept in the common house.

The marks slept under cloth.

The cord did not sleep. I could tell. It lay too straight.

That night every house spoke the warm names longer than usual. My mother said seed three times. My father said door twice and touched the latch each time. My grandmother said soil with both hands on the floor. When she said blood, she looked at me and then away.

I asked if someone had died.

"No," my mother said.

"Then why are we using the names for keeping?"

"Because names like to be used," she said.

That was true, but it was not an answer.

The singers came to the common house before dawn. They sang the lamp song low so they would not wake the watchers too roughly. At the place where my mother had stopped, three singers stopped. One sang return. One sang carried. One did not sing at all.

The watchers opened their eyes.

They did not cry. They ate bread. They thanked the elders. One smiled at a child near the door, and the child hid because the smile was too careful.

I was behind the water jar. I had not been called. I knew this meant I was almost allowed to listen.

The oldest elder asked no quick question. The makers of measures sat apart. The undertakers stood at the wall. The seed keepers held their hands closed as if each palm had a sleeping seed inside.

I thought of the song. I thought of the word carried. I thought of a lamp walking with a bundle on its back. I thought of a house far away where a lamp might knock and say, I am lost, let me in.

So I asked it.

"Did the lost lamp find its house?"

Every watcher turned toward me.

Not one after another. Not because I was in trouble. All together, as if a cord had pulled their faces.

Then I knew I had carried something in my mouth.

My mother came to take me away, but the elder lifted one hand. So I stayed behind the water jar while the whole room became quiet around my question.

No one answered.

That frightened me more than shouting.

At night the council began. Children old enough to remember were called to sit near the floor. We were told to keep our hands where elders could see them. I did not know why hands mattered. Later I thought maybe a hidden hand can carry a mark, or a stone, or a new word.

The watchers said their seeing had outrun their singing.

I did not know how seeing could run. I imagined an eye with feet and did not laugh.

The undertakers said the graves felt too small under the widened sky. I looked at my grandmother because her mother was in the north ground. My grandmother's face did not change. Her hands moved over the warm names without sound.

The singers said a line had changed while they sang it. I understood this. Sometimes a song changes in your mouth before you choose it. Usually that is funny. No one laughed.

Then the children asked questions.

One asked if the Creator had moved the lamps.

One asked if the lamps had always been far.

One asked if looking had hurt them.

One asked whether a thing not looked at could stay kind.

The elders did not answer quickly. Their slowness was a cloth over a wound, but I could still see the blood at the edge.

On the last night a cord, a covered lamp, and a bowl of old soil were put in the center. We children were allowed to put our hands on the floor. The floor was warm from so many bodies. I wanted to believe the soil in the bowl could hear the soil under the house.

The elder said no one thing would rule alone.

I said the warm names in my head so the lamp names would not come first.

After the council, the song changed.

It did not change all at once. Songs do not like being dragged. First we sang seed, hearth, salt, door, mother, blood, morning, soil. Then we took one breath. Then the singers gave us the names of lamps that had no old place in the verse.

We were told never to say the far names before the warm names.

We were told never to ask a lamp question in a house where someone had not eaten.

We were told never to let one watcher climb alone.

These were called old rules, though I remembered when they were born.

Years later, a little child sat beside me during a rain night. Thunder walked over the roof. I wrapped him in the sleeping cloth that smelled of smoke, milk, and old skin.

He asked, "If we had not looked, would the lamps have stayed near?"

I put my thumb near his mouth, but I did not stop him. A stopped question can grow teeth.

I said, "Seed."

He said, "Seed."

I said, "Hearth."

He said, "Hearth."

We spoke salt, door, mother, blood, morning, and soil. I made him touch each thing that could be touched. Only after that did I teach him the far lamp names.

When he slept, I listened to the rain and tried to remember whether I had been protected as a child, or only taught to carry the danger gently.

## Translator's Note

This child manuscript presents instruction as protection. I have preserved its short sequence of warm names before lamp names, although the order may also encode a rule for controlled transmission.

The question about the lost lamp is treated as innocent inside the tale. I am less certain. The surrounding witnesses imply that the child's question made the room recognize what had already spread. "Child" may mean a young person, a novice, or a class of hearers not yet authorized to hold the first knowledge.

I have not regularized "house" into orbit, station, grave, or assigned place. The manuscript needs the word to remain small enough for a child and large enough to accuse the adults.
