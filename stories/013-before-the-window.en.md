# Before the Window

> The window was covered before longing reached it. The name was closed before the window. Later copies disagree on whether this was prudence, fear, or the first mercy of the course-keepers.

I signed the order to close the windows with a steady hand.

That is how the record keeps me.

It does not keep the sweat under my collar, or the way I had to press my thumb against the stylus because the table had begun to tremble. It does not keep the apprentice beside me counting too loudly. It does not keep the old woman at the back of the course-house asking whether a covered window could still be called a window.

The record says: all outward observation ports covered for synchronization protection.

It is a clean sentence.

I trusted clean sentences in those days. A ship cannot cross dark on feeling. It cannot be steered by songs except in the old ceremonial sense, and even then only because the songs keep the counters awake. Course is number made obedient. Course is number taught not to become wish.

The black well was already bending the tables before anyone called it holy.

Not by much.

That was the danger.

A disaster that announces itself in broken beams and screaming metal can be answered by every body at once. A small drift is more intimate. It comes to the wrist. It makes one keeper lean over a slate longer than another. It turns a correction into an opinion.

At third watch the aft clocks were late by less than a breath. At fourth watch the sleepers in the west preservation room began to breathe against the common count, not far enough to wake the nurses, far enough that the nurse who loved one of them touched the glass twice.

By fifth watch the people had found the word well.

No one knows who spoke it first. The chart called it a black mass until the ink was revised. The older tables called it a sink. The engineers called it a bounded loss until the elders asked whether loss should be named before anything had been taken.

Well was softer.

Well gave the mouth a rim to stand at.

Once the word spread, people came to the course-house with excuses. A grain steward needed the outer temperature. A singer needed to check the lamps. A child had lost a counting bead and believed it had rolled under the watching bench. None of them looked at me when they spoke. All of them looked at the covered wall.

"It is not covered because it is forbidden," I told them.

That was true.

"It is covered because the ship must keep one time."

That was also true.

Truths can cooperate badly.

The saint came after the second set of knots was inspected.

He was not yet the saint then. He was a quiet man whose work had been to settle quarrels over food measures and sleeping shifts. People trusted him because he did not use gentleness to hurry them. He had the dangerous patience of a door left open.

He stopped before the main window. Cloth, cord, seal, my mark.

"I will not ask you to uncover it," he said.

"Good."

"I will stand here a while."

"The course-house is closed to nonessential presence."

"Then mark me as a load."

I thought he was joking. He was not. He held out his wrist so the apprentice could read the work-band. His mass, height, circulation count, ration needs, sleeping allotment, expected error under strain: all the small humiliations by which the ship allowed a person to occupy space.

"No load stands by a window," I said.

"Then mark me as a witness."

"Witnesses observe."

"Not always."

His calm made my refusal sound like anger. I hated him a little for that before I knew I would need the hatred.

I sent for the council.

That was later described as my attempt to suppress him. It may have been. It was also what the manual required when a passenger action, symbolic or procedural, risked altering course discipline. The manual did not define symbolic. It did not need to. On a long voyage, anything that gathers enough eyes becomes a mass.

The council met between the cooled-name rooms and the course-house, as if grief and direction could balance each other by standing on either side of a table.

I brought the drift sheets.

The grave keepers brought their sealed leaves.

The namekeepers brought dark thread.

The saint brought nothing in his hands.

This made him look innocent.

I spoke first because the ship was mine while the well was near. Not mine by honor. Mine by blame.

"No window opens," I said. "No exterior call is made. No name is lowered into the outward channel. No correction is delayed for ritual, argument, or grief. We pass without adding meaning to the object."

An elder asked, "Can meaning be added from outside?"

"Meaning changes hands," I said. "Hands change switches."

Some laughed softly. They thought I had made a dry answer. I had given them the plainest fear I owned.

The saint bowed to me before he spoke.

I wish he had not.

"Let the windows remain closed," he said. "Let the ship keep its road. I ask for no answer and no turn. I ask only that a question be laid where no council can make use of it."

"A question placed in an outward channel is a signal," I said.

"Then call it a signal that expects no return."

"Return is not the only danger."

He looked at me then, directly enough that I could not hide behind the sheets.

"I know."

That was when my anger faltered. I had expected longing. I had prepared for holy hunger, for people who want danger to prove their courage. He knew the risk was not only death, not only the well, not only the unknown pressure on the hull.

He knew a meaning could bend a ship before gravity did.

He knew and still asked.

The grave keeper refused him for the dead. I refused him for the road. The elders refused him for the living, then softened the refusal until it had corners no one could grip.

No open window.

No course change.

No name that could still be warmed by a living household.

No call-back.

No public hearing.

No second attempt.

The saint accepted each condition as if we were building the thing he had come to request.

That frightened me more than disobedience would have.

Before the council closed, I added one more condition.

"Your own name must be removed from the active call table."

A murmur went through the room. The grave keeper looked at me with the first approval she had ever given me. The elders grew still.

The saint only asked, "Before or after?"

"Before."

"Then the ship is not being asked to retrieve me."

"The ship is not being asked at all."

He smiled once, not happily.

"You do understand."

I wanted to deny it.

Instead I signed the supplement.

The next watch was all instruments.

That is what I tell myself when I remember it. Instruments, corrections, lamps dimmed to work brightness, cords checked, assistants assigned in pairs so no hand could act alone. I set the aft clock by the forward clock and the forward clock by the median pulse. I ordered the west preservation room to use manual breath count until the drift settled.

I did not go to the namekeepers' chamber when he signed himself out of recall.

I saw the copy later.

His hand was smaller on paper than I expected. The line beneath it read: Do not call back for comfort.

The apprentice asked whether that meant the saint expected to die.

"It means he has denied us a procedure," I said.

"Is that allowed?"

"We allowed it."

He went quiet after that.

The act itself took place behind covered windows.

People later insisted there must have been light. A black light, some said. A pressure in the bones. A voice without sound. They needed the moment to show itself, because otherwise the next part was unbearable: a man, three officers, two sealed leaves, and no visible sign that the universe had noticed.

I stood at the course table with my back to the main window.

Not because I was brave.

Because if I turned, the apprentice would turn. If the apprentice turned, the outer assistants would turn. If they turned, the people beyond the rail would lean. Bodies make a vote before mouths do.

The first input was made.

The broken Creator-name, if the namekeepers' later account is trusted.

My table showed no change beyond expected drift.

The second input was made.

A small bundle of cooled names, no living household attached.

The aft clock lost another fraction. I corrected it manually. My hand obeyed. I remember being grateful to my hand as if it were another person.

The third input was made.

His chosen name.

No one spoke it in the course-house. No one was permitted to. Still, I felt the room make room for a syllable it had not heard.

That is not a navigational statement.

I do not know what kind of statement it is.

The forward alarms remained silent. The hull load stayed within tolerance. The sleepers in the west room returned slowly to common breath. The covered windows did not brighten, darken, open, crack, or bleed cold through the seals.

Nothing returned.

Because nothing returned, no one knew where to place blame.

The saint stood alive after it was done, if alive means that the body continued to answer gravity and breath. He placed both hands on the sealed leaves, then removed them. He looked once toward the covered window. He did not ask to see.

That, more than the vow, nearly made me open it.

Not for him.

For myself.

I wanted one glance to prove that all my refusing had faced a thing outside us and not only a hunger inside the ship.

The apprentice said, "Course?"

I wrote: no additional drift.

Not success. Not failure. Not answered. Not silenced.

No additional drift.

It was the smallest true sentence available to me.

After the well passed beyond the dangerous angle, the people wanted rules. They wanted a shape they could teach without retelling the whole fear. The elders asked the grave keepers. The grave keepers asked the namekeepers. The namekeepers asked me to write the first line because the first covering had borne my mark.

I delayed for one watch.

Then two.

On the third, I wrote:

When approaching a black well, close the name before the window.

The elders accepted it.

The grave keepers said it protected the dead.

The namekeepers said it protected the living.

The singers said it honored the saint.

I said it protected the course.

All of us may have been using the same sentence to hide from one another.

Years later, when younger course-keepers asked why the name must be closed first, I gave them the official answer. A named longing will seek a surface. A surface will seek an opening. An opening will seek a correction. A correction made for longing is no correction.

They wrote it down.

The best students always wrote it down.

One asked, "Was the saint wrong?"

I had trained them not to ask that sort of question in the course-house. The course-house accepts useful questions, timed questions, questions whose answers alter procedure. But the well had taught me that refusing a question does not remove its mass.

"The ship survived him," I said.

"That is not the same."

"No."

She waited.

"The ship survived me too."

That answer did not satisfy her, but it kept the room steady.

I still dream of the covered window.

In the dream I am always standing before it, not at the table. My hands are empty. The cords are intact. Behind me the ship breathes in one count, beautiful and obedient. Before me is cloth. Behind the cloth is dark, or nothing, or a bend in every word that has ever tried to travel straight.

I do not lift the cover.

I do not call.

I do not pray.

I place my palm against the cloth to make sure it holds.

Sometimes that is safety.

Sometimes it is censorship.

Most nights, the ship cannot tell the difference.

Neither can I.

## Translator's Note

This navigator witness treats prayer as an unauthorized outward operation, but the text never lets that explanation settle the event. The course-keeper's language may preserve the saint's act in a form the ship could survive, or it may be the earliest surviving method for suppressing it.

The rule "close the name before the window" is therefore double-edged. It can be read as practical protection against synchronization drift, as mercy toward names that should not be used for longing, or as a discipline that prevents future witnesses from learning what the saint actually attempted.
