# The First Manuscript: Under the Firmament

## I. Life Under the Covered Sky

Before the first knowledge, before the far lamps troubled the songs, the people lived beneath the firmament and did not yet call it by a hard name. They called it roof when rain struck it softly and returned as blessing. They called it height when smoke rose from the hearth and lost itself before fear began. They called it covering when night came down with far lamps in order, each one praised without strain.

The Middle Land rested under that sky as bread rests under cloth before the meal is shared. The fields were broad enough for quarrel and forgiveness. The roads were long enough for a child to become tired and short enough for the child's name to be heard from the doorway. The wells answered by their stones. The graves answered by their grasses. The houses answered one another with smoke, open doors, and the sound of tools being set down at evening.

No one said that the world was small. Smallness had not yet been separated from care. The people knew the hill where the sun first touched the roofs. They knew the low field where frost waited longest. They knew which path avoided the sleeping stones and which spring ran bitter after storm. This was enough for sowing, enough for judgment, enough for the first song after mourning.

The elders taught that a covered thing is not always hidden. Grain is covered until it is needed. A child is covered against cold. A lamp is covered when wind would shame its flame. So the sky covered the Middle Land, and the people received the covering as mercy. If the covering also withheld, that withholding had not yet become a wound in any mouth.

## II. Near Names and Warm Names

In those days, names were near. Seed was near to the hand that planted it. Hearth was near to the smoke on the sleeve. Salt was near to the tongue and to the debt of shared bread. Door was near to the one who crossed it and the one who waited beside it. Mother, blood, morning, soil: each name answered from a body, a face, a weight, or a place where knees had pressed the earth.

The children learned these names before the names of far lamps. They learned them in the order of touch. First the grain in the palm. Then the fire that must not be struck in anger. Then the threshold where a guest became answerable. Then the grave where the voice was lowered but not silenced. The singers did not hurry them toward distant things. A mouth that cannot bless the near will only wound the far.

When a name was spoken, something turned toward the speaker. The cow lifted its head. The field remembered the feet that had crossed it. The dead did not rise, but the place above them grew attentive. Even a tool, if called by its household name, seemed less likely to cut wrongly. The people did not mistake this for command. They called it warmth.

There were already names that cooled at the edges. A great-grandmother known only by a cradle song. A stream whose old bend had filled with reeds. A law remembered after the reason for it had gone quiet. The namekeepers did not despise these names. They placed them beside warm names during the feasts and said, "What cools may yet be held. What is held may yet answer, if not with warmth, then with distinction."

Thus the people were taught that nearness was not possession. A name was warm because it could answer, not because it could be seized. The warm name belonged to the called one first, then to the household, then to the field of hearing, then to the singers who carried it after the body had gone down into soil.

## III. When Measure Still Served

Measure also was near. It lay in cords, bowls, fingers, steps, and shadows. It stood beside the granary door to keep hunger from taking more than hunger needed. It crossed a field so that seed might be scattered with care. It marked a grave so the dead would not be crowded beneath the names that loved them. It counted far lamps so praise would not grow careless.

No one feared the measuring rod when it returned to the wall after its work. No one cursed the cord when it was rolled and hung beside the door. A good measure ended where its duty ended. It did not follow the eye into distance. It did not ask the far lamp why it burned. It did not ask the rim of the sky what held it up. It served the hand and then rested.

The makers of measures were therefore honored, but not placed above the singers. The singers were honored, but not placed above the undertakers. The undertakers were honored, but not placed above the seed stewards. Each calling knew the portion entrusted to it. Bread was cut by measure, grief was ordered by song, seed was kept by patient hands, and the dead were laid down with enough room for love to kneel beside them.

When disputes came, the elders brought cord, stone, water, and bread. The cord showed where the path had moved. The stone showed where the oath had been placed. The water cooled the mouth before judgment. The bread reminded the angry that hunger had no clan. The measure did not decide alone. It gave the quarrel a shape small enough for mercy to enter.

So measure was praised as a servant that knew how to stop. Later mouths say that measure had not yet been wounded. The old singers say it more gently: the measure still came home.

## IV. The Teaching Not to Ask the Rim

The rim of the sky was taught to children as the place where sight rested. "Do not chase the eye beyond its bed," the elders said. "Let the far lamp go to its appointed house. Let the hill keep the last light. Let the night finish its own garment." This was not spoken as fear. It was spoken as table law, field law, burial law: enough has a holy border.

The watchers of the rim were not forbidden to look. They were appointed to look with companions, songs, and clean mouths. They counted the far lamps for the planting of seed and the mending of roofs. They kept the old returns so no season would arrive without greeting. When they came down from the rises, they washed, ate, and placed their marks before the singers. The marks became verses, and the verses returned to the children as weather, harvest, and praise.

Still, the elders warned against lonely looking. One eye alone grows proud or frightened. One mouth alone makes a wound larger by naming it too quickly. If a child asked where the sky rested, the elder did not strike the child. The elder placed the child's hand on soil, then on water, then on the doorpost, and said, "Here is where your seeing returns today."

Some say the Creator gave this teaching. Some say the closed sky itself taught it by receiving sight and giving sight back. Some say the elders made the teaching after older griefs had been covered over. The received recitation does not choose. It keeps the teaching because it preserved peace for many generations, and because peace, though covered, was not nothing.

Therefore the people did not ask the rim as one asks an enemy. They honored it as they honored a threshold: crossed only when called, touched before crossing, never mocked from either side.

## V. The Small Unease

Yet even under the firmament, not every silence was sleep. There were nights when the far lamps returned correctly and still a watcher kept one hand over the marks. There were mornings when a warm name answered, but the answer came a breath late. There were graves whose grasses bent though no foot had passed, and children who woke saying that the roof of the sky had lifted while everyone else slept.

The elders did not make a terror of these things. They set bread on the table. They asked the watchers to stand in threes. They told the singers to repeat the names of seed, hearth, salt, door, mother, blood, morning, and soil before teaching any new far lamp name. They sent the undertakers among the graves to warm the old names with water and ash.

For a time, this was enough. The far lamps kept their places in the songs. The fields received seed. The roads returned feet to doorways. The graves received speech. The children grew, quarreled, married, and buried their own dead under the same covering mercy.

But the covering had begun to feel thin to certain eyes. The rim still received sight, yet some sight came back carrying cold. The measure still came home, yet some makers of measures stared at their own cords as if the cord had crossed a place no hand could follow. The warm names still warmed, yet the namekeepers began to leave a space beside them for the names that might need cooling.

The old reciters do not call this fall. They do not call it gift. They say only that the people were being prepared for a knowledge they had not asked to carry. The closed sky had not yet withdrawn. The Middle Land had not yet become ship. The measuring rods had not yet been broken beneath the common fire. Still, in the mouth of one child, after the evening names had been spoken, a question remained awake.

"If the sky is covering us," the child asked, "what covers the sky?"

The elders answered first with silence, then with bread, then with the warm names, because this was the mercy they still knew how to give.

## Translator's Note

Firmament renders a term also translated in later chapters as closed sky. The Japanese 天蓋 may suggest a canopy, a made heaven, a limit of sight, or a censored boundary. The First Manuscript does not require the reader to choose among these senses.

The phrase rim of the sky likewise remains unstable. In the myth body it is the place where sight rests and returns; in later interpretation it may point toward an artificial celestial sphere, an observation limit, a form of knowing, or a boundary deliberately left unnamed.

This chapter appears to precede the First Knowledge tradition. Its restraint should not be mistaken for simple nostalgia. The covered sky is remembered as mercy, but the same language leaves room for protection, confinement, permitted ignorance, and withheld sight.
