# The First Manuscript: The Birth of the Covered World

## I. Before the First Roof

Before the first knowledge, before the Middle Land was named middle, before the far lamps were counted into their houses, there was no field for a foot and no door for a hand. There was depth without road, brightness without morning, and dark without the mercy of night.

The old singers do not say that nothing was there. They say rather that nothing had yet been gathered close enough to answer. Light had not learned softness. Soil had not learned weight. Water had not learned the cup. Breath had not learned the mouth. Name had not yet found the one who could turn toward it.

Then the Creator, or the Mercy that stands where the Creator's name has been washed thin, drew a covering over the unheld dark. The covering was not a wall raised in anger. It was a roof spread before fear had a house. It was a cloth laid over bread before the meal. It was a hand curved over a lamp so that the flame might become useful before it was asked to be brave.

Under that covering, distance was made gentle. Above it, the singers lower their voices. They do not say whether there was above, or whether above was a word born later when children learned to point. They say only that the covering gave a first within, and that within the first within, the world could begin to be called.

## II. The Softening of Light

The first light was too keen for names. It crossed everything and rested nowhere. It did not yet bless a face, mark a threshold, or return from water in a form that a child could laugh toward. Therefore the covering received the light and softened it.

The softened light did not boast of its beginning. It came down as morning, warmed the back of the hand, entered smoke, showed dust in the doorway, and withdrew in order so that sleep would not be shamed. The people later called it mercy because it made seeing possible without demanding that all things be seen.

With softened light came the first shadows, and the first shadows were not yet feared. Shadow lay beside stone so stone could be known by shape. Shadow lay under the eaves so a child could rest there. Shadow lay in the furrow so seed could wait without being accused of hiding.

The far lamps were then set, or seen, or remembered as set. They stood in the covering like small vows. Each had enough brightness to be praised and not enough to command the field. The watchers had not yet been appointed, but praise was already learning to count without wounding what it counted.

## III. The Wet Soil

When light had been softened, the wet soil was gathered. It was not dry dust, for dry dust scatters from every breath. It was not deep water, for deep water gives no place for a hearth. It was soil with enough water to receive a print and enough weight to keep it.

The soil was called good because it answered slowly. A hand pressed it, and it kept the hand's hollow for a little while. A seed entered it, and it did not return the seed unchanged. A knee bent into it, and the knee rose with a mark. Later generations praised quickness, but the first world was saved by things that did not answer too quickly.

From the wet soil came the first measures that did not wound. A furrow could be drawn. A stone could be placed. A grave could be prepared before grief knew the one who would need it. A bowl could be shaped and filled. A threshold could be raised from mud, timber, and promise.

The soil held warmth without claiming it. It received rain without becoming only water. It bore root, worm, salt, ash, and the first small bones of creatures whose names are no longer warm. The old singers say the soil was humble. The undertakers say it was patient. The seed stewards say it was faithful because it could be opened and still remain itself.

## IV. The Middle Land Raised

Then the Middle Land was raised within the covering. It was not placed at the center as a king is placed above servants. It was set in the middle as bread is set where many hands may reach, as a fire is set where each face may receive some warmth, as a grave is set where descendants may pass without forgetting.

Hills rose so sight could rest and return. Rivers bent so feet could follow them and come home. Fields spread broad enough for labor and narrow enough for blessing. Roads learned the distance between houses. Stones learned the patience of border. Wells learned to answer with coldness that refreshed rather than refused.

The covering held the Middle Land near, and nearness was not yet a chain. The rim of the sky gave sight a bed. The far lamps gave praise its order. The softened light gave morning its hand. The wet soil gave every name a place where it might be spoken toward weight.

When the Middle Land first breathed, the breath did not belong to one mouth. Smoke rose from hearths not yet built. Rain remembered roofs not yet raised. Graves waited without hunger. Seeds slept without fear. The world had become ready for those who would quarrel, forgive, plant, bury, sing, measure, and be measured.

## V. The First Near Names

After the Middle Land was raised, the first near names were spoken. Seed was spoken toward the furrow. Hearth was spoken toward the place where fire would be kept. Salt was spoken toward the tongue and toward the debt of shared bread. Door was spoken toward the crossing between guest and household. Mother, blood, morning, soil: each name was given a place to answer from.

The names did not seize the things they called. They warmed them. When morning was named, light returned with gentleness. When soil was named, the ground bore the knee without contempt. When door was named, the threshold learned both welcome and refusal. When grave was named, the earth received sorrow without swallowing the whole song.

The elders teach that the first world was covered so that names could become warm before they were asked to travel. A name sent too soon into the unheld dark becomes cold before any mouth can learn it. A light left unsoftened burns the eye that would praise it. Soil left ungathered cannot keep the print of love.

Therefore the covered world was born not as a prison, nor as a possession, but as a first mercy. What was within could answer. What answered could be named. What was named could be loved without being wholly understood.

This is why later reciters bless the covering before they bless the voyage, and bless the wet soil before they bless the ship. The ship carried what the covered world first taught to be near. The voyage preserved what the softened light and patient soil had first allowed to answer.

So the singers end with the old formula for beginnings: May the covering be remembered without hatred. May the light be softened without deceit. May the soil remain wet enough to receive us. May the Middle Land be called middle not because all else is nothing, but because there the first answering was gathered close.

## Translator's Note

This chapter is placed before the traditions of the firmament and the First Knowledge. It presents creation as enclosure, softening, and gathering rather than conquest or manufacture.

The phrases covered world, softened light, and wet soil should not be narrowed too quickly. They may preserve memories of physical history, prepared environment, edited remembrance, or the first boundary within which a knowable world could appear. The First Manuscript renders these as mercies because its concern is communal preservation.

The birth of the Middle Land is likewise unstable. It may name an origin place, an artificial habitation, an early condition of the cosmos, or the forming of an observable world around those who could later call it home. The chapter does not decide whether the covering protected the first people from the dark, from knowledge, or from a world not yet fit to be named.
