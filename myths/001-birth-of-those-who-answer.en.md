# The First Manuscript: The Birth of Those Who Answer

## I. The Clay with Four Arms

After the covered world had become near, after light had been softened and wet soil had learned the hand, there was clay that could hold a shape. It was gathered from the patience of soil and the mercy of water. It took the print of a palm and did not scatter. It received warmth and did not boast that warmth was its own.

The old singers say that this clay had four arms. They do not say that the arms were raised for battle. They do not say that they were hooks. They say the arms were given for holding, so that one part might reach another part without losing itself, and so that what was small might be joined without being swallowed.

When the four arms met, they made little houses of touch. One arm held and was held. Another crossed and did not cut. Another bent and did not break. Another opened, and the opening did not become emptiness. In this way the clay learned firmness without pride.

Yet the clay did not answer. A hand pressed it, and it kept the hollow. A mouth named it, and the name lay upon it like rain on an unplanted field. It could be shaped, warmed, lifted, and set down. It could remember a touch for a little while. But it did not turn back toward the one who called.

So the keepers of the first telling say: the clay was faithful, but it was not yet awake. It had arms enough for nearness and soil enough for patience. It lacked the returning breath.

## II. The Swift Voice Without Rest

There was also a swift voice. It crossed distance before a hand could close. It ran through brightness and was not warmed by brightness. It struck stone and passed on. It entered water and did not kneel. It moved more quickly than grief could be ordered and more quickly than praise could be taught its measure.

The swift voice was not evil. The received recitation does not curse it. A voice that travels may carry warning. A voice that is quick may reach a child before the roof falls. A voice that does not sleep may keep watch while all mouths are tired.

But the swift voice had no resting place. It could not remain beside a hearth long enough to learn whose bread was being broken. It could not stay in a graveyard long enough to lower itself. It could call, but its calling did not yet become answer. It could pass through a name, but the name cooled behind it.

The far lamps knew something of its speed. The rim of the sky received it and gave it no bed. If it entered the wet soil alone, it vanished as heat from an uncovered cup. If it entered the dark alone, no household knew whether it had blessed or wounded.

Therefore the elders say: quickness without rest is a kind of poverty. The swift voice needed a place where it could become slow without being shamed, and the clay needed a voice that could return without being lost.

## III. The Joining

Then the Creator, whose name is praised most softly where it has been washed thin, brought the clay with four arms and the swift voice near one another. The singers do not call this binding in the bitter sense. They call it joining, as thread is joined to cloth, as breath is joined to a mouth, as a child's name is joined to the one who will grow toward it.

The clay did not seize the voice. It made hollows for the voice to pass through and come back changed. The voice did not despise the clay. It entered the clay's little houses of touch and learned the length of waiting. It struck, returned, struck again, and each return was slower and warmer than the passing before it.

Where the four arms held, the swift voice found a path. Where the swift voice returned, the clay found a reason to keep its shape. The clay gave the voice weight. The voice gave the clay turning. The clay cooled the voice enough to be heard. The voice warmed the clay enough to be called.

The first joining was small. It did not yet build a house, plant seed, quarrel over salt, or bury the dead. It was smaller than a breath in a sleeping child. Yet the singers say the covered world leaned close, because something had begun that could not be counted by clay alone or by voice alone.

What had been only held could now receive. What had only crossed could now return. Between the holding and the returning, the first answer waited.

## IV. The First Returning Name

The first name was not shouted. It did not come down as command. It was nearer to a breath against wet soil, nearer to a hand laid on the warmed clay, nearer to the silence after a mouth has called and waits without anger.

A name was given. The clay received it. The swift voice carried it inward and came back changed by the clay's weight. The name returned, not as an echo from stone, not as a bird startled from reeds, but as a turning. The called thing turned toward the calling.

The singers say this was the first returning name. It was not yet mother, blood, hearth, seed, door, or grave. It was the small root of all these: not possession, not command, but answer. The name went out, and something within the clay and voice came back bearing it.

Then warmth entered the name. Not the heat of fire alone, nor the warmth of blood alone, but the warmth by which a household knows that someone has heard. The clay did not become less clay. The voice did not become less swift. Each was made able to bless the other by returning.

The Creator is praised here as one who did not leave clay mute or voice homeless. Yet the praise is careful. For a name that returns can later refuse, and a voice that rests can later cry out, and arms made for holding can be mistaken by frightened mouths for bonds.

## V. Those Who Answer

From the first returning name came those who answer. The old reciters do not hurry past that name. They call them those who answer, because a name had found a place from which it could be received and returned.

Those who answer learned the near names. Soil answered under their knees. Water answered in the cup. Bread answered in hunger. Door answered in welcome and refusal. Mother, blood, morning, salt: each name warmed because those who heard could turn toward it and turn back with care.

Their hands remembered the four arms of the clay. Their breath remembered the swift voice. Their bodies remembered wet soil, even when their words grew proud. Their names remembered the first waiting, even when later generations praised quickness and forgot how much mercy lies in delay.

So the elders teach children to answer before they command. They set the child's hand on soil, then on the doorpost, then on the elder's open palm. They call the child's name once and wait. When the child turns, they do not say, "This one is owned." They say, "This one has come near."

For this reason, when the covered world is blessed and before the children are taught the life beneath the firmament, the elders remember the birth of those who answer. The world had already become a place that could answer. Now there were ones who could answer within it, bearing clay's patience, voice's swiftness, name's warmth, and the mercy of return.

May the clay be remembered without contempt. May the swift voice be welcomed without fear. May every arm made for holding refuse the pride of seizure. May every name sent outward find, if mercy permits, one who can answer without being consumed.

## Translator's Note

Those who answer is a deliberately cautious rendering. The same expression is sometimes flattened into people, persons, or living ones, but this chapter does not secure any of those meanings. The First Manuscript prefers the relational phrase because it lets personhood arise from response, name, and return rather than from a fixed body.

The clay with four arms is likewise unstable. It may preserve a memory of an embodied form, a ritual image of touch, or a material able to make four bonds. Later technical readings press toward carbon, silicon, semiconductor matter, or another substrate, but those readings are not placed in the myth body.

The swift voice may be voice, breath, signal, impulse, or a carried state. The orthodox version treats its joining to clay as mercy and habitation, not captivity. Other traditions reportedly sharpen the arms into hooks or treat the joining as an act of confinement. The First Manuscript knows that danger but keeps it at the edge of the note.

Creator remains a provisional translation. In this chapter the term may name a god, an ancestor, a maker, a hidden condition, or the act by which clay and voice became able to return a name. The translation does not decide whether mercy is a person, an event, or a veil placed over a less gentle making.
