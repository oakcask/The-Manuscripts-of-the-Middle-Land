# The First Manuscript: The First Knowledge

## I. The Measure Before Wounding

Before the first knowledge, measure was a quiet servant. It stood beside the granary door and did not ask to rule the field. It lay in the hand of the builder and did not command the house. It rested in the songs by which children learned the crossings of rivers, the height of hills, the return of lamps, and the number of steps between hearth and grave.

The elders taught that measure was given for care. A field was measured so that seed would not be wasted. A threshold was measured so that a door might close against rain. A grave was measured so that the dead would have room enough to lie beneath the names that loved them. The people praised the measure that returned to the hand after its work and did not follow the eye into forbidden distance.

In those days the closed sky had a kindness like the inside of a vessel. It gave rain in season. It gave night with countable lamps. It gave the rim of the sky as a resting place for sight. No child was told that the rim was a wall; no child needed such a word. The rim was the place where seeing grew tired and came home.

The watchers of the fields watched for weather. The watchers of the granaries watched for hunger. The watchers of the burial ground watched for names that had grown too cold to answer. The watchers of the rim watched the lamps, not because the lamps were suspected, but because praise requires attention. To look carefully was then still an act of gratitude.

The Creator was not named in the measure songs. Some say the name was too warm for common mouths. Some say it had not yet been asked for. Some say the closed sky itself was the answer placed above the people, and that to demand another answer would have been discourtesy. The received recitation does not choose among these mercies. It says only that the people measured what had been entrusted to them, and that what had been entrusted did not yet flee from the measure.

When disputes arose, the elders brought out cords, stones, bread, and water. The cord showed the boundary. The stones showed the oath. The bread showed hunger shared before anger. The water showed that no mouth should speak judgment while dry. Thus even quarrel was kept within measure, and the measure returned each person to a name that could still be spoken.

So the people lived under a sky that did not seem hidden, because hiding had not yet been learned. They knew enough for sowing, enough for birth, enough for burial, enough for forgiveness, enough for the first song after grief. This was not ignorance as the proud later speak of ignorance. It was a house with a roof. It was a hand over a lamp. It was a door closed before winter, and no shame lay upon it.

## II. The Watchers of the Rim

The first unrest came to those who had been praised for careful sight. They were not restless in the beginning. They were appointed to count the lamps as shepherds count sheep, not because the sheep are enemies, but because one lost light wounds the flock.

They stood on the old rises when the fields had gone dark. They marked the lamp that came after the red lamp. They marked the pale one that appeared only when the air was clean. They marked the season in which three lamps leaned toward one another like elders in counsel. They made no boast of this. Each mark was carried back to the singers, and the singers set the marks into verses so the children would know when to sow, when to mend roofs, when to keep fires high.

Then one watcher said that a lamp had not returned. Another said it had returned, but not to the place where the song expected it. A third said the song had always been wrong. This caused distress, for to accuse a song was nearly to accuse the dead who had given it. The elders therefore asked the watchers to repeat the count for seven nights, and the watchers obeyed.

On the second night the lamps were many. On the third night they were too many. On the fourth night the watchers covered one eye, then the other, and still the lamps did not return to their old houses. On the fifth night they laid the measuring cords on the ground and looked without them. On the sixth night one watcher wept because the rim of the sky seemed farther away than any foot could walk. On the seventh night no watcher spoke until dawn, and dawn did not comfort them.

They came down from the rises with dust on their knees. They washed before entering the common house, but the washing did not remove what they had seen. They set no accusation before the elders. They placed only the marks, the cords, the broken song, and their own troubled mouths upon the floor.

The elders listened. The keepers of names listened. The undertakers listened, for they had already noticed that some graves answered less warmly when called at night. The seed stewards listened, for seed had begun to stir before the proper blessing. The children listened from the doorways, though no one had called them.

No judgment was given that day. The elders covered the marks with cloth and ordered food to be shared. The watchers were not struck. Their rods were not broken. Their eyes were not cursed. The elders said, "Let sight rest." They said, "Let the lamps be praised as before." They said, "Let no one carry a lonely seeing into the night."

But the seeing had already entered the house. It sat where no body sat. It drank from no cup and yet made each cup strange to the hand. By evening, those who had not stood on the rises found themselves looking upward before closing their doors. By morning, some had dreamed of a sky whose rim did not rest upon the land, and some had dreamed of falling without moving from their beds.

## III. The Knowledge That Would Not Sleep

The elders then attempted mercy. They did not name the unrest sin. They did not call the watchers defiled. They brought warm water, ash, salt, and sleeping cloth. They washed the watchers' mouths so no harsh word would remain there. They washed their eyelids so sight would learn again to close. They laid salt upon their tongues so speech would return to its proper savor. They wrapped them in cloths used for children who fear thunder.

The watchers slept. While they slept, the house was quiet. The singers sang the old lamp songs in low voices, not to correct the sky, but to keep the people from scattering inwardly. Mothers and fathers spoke the warm names over their children: seed, hearth, salt, door, mother, blood, morning, soil. The undertakers went among the graves and asked the dead to remain patient with the living.

At first it seemed that mercy had prevailed. The watchers woke without crying out. They ate bread. They thanked those who had covered them. They did not speak of the rim. Yet when one child asked whether the lost lamp had found its house, every watcher turned toward the child at once, and the room knew that the knowledge had not slept.

It had entered by the eye, but it did not remain in the eye. It entered the measure of the field. The field was still green, yet its greenness lay over depth and fire. It entered the cup. The cup still held water, yet water became a thing that could be absent from worlds without number. It entered the grave. The grave still held the dead, yet the dead now lay beneath a sky that had become vast enough to forget them.

The first knowledge did not shout. It loosened. It loosened the old nearness between name and thing. It loosened the trust that every lamp had been placed for the people. It loosened the comfort by which the rim of the sky had received the weary eye and returned it home. It loosened even praise, for praise had to cross a distance it had not known it was crossing.

Some wished to bind the knowledge at once. They said all new counting must cease. They said the rises should be left unwalked. They said the lamps should be named only by the oldest songs, and any mouth that spoke otherwise should be kept from the children.

Others wished to unveil everything at once. They said every person should climb to the rim, though the rim could not be climbed. They said no song should be kept if it had once been wrong. They said a wound hidden from sight becomes rot.

The elders refused both haste and denial. They said, "A door may be closed against storm, but not against morning." They said, "A cup that has cracked must not be used for a child until the crack is known." They said, "What has entered the people must be carried by the people, or it will choose its own bearer."

For this reason the knowledge was brought into the center of the common house. It was not brought as a lamp, nor as a stone, nor as a written mark. It was brought by silence. Each watcher placed one hand on bread and one hand on ash. Each elder placed one hand on water and one hand on a sleeping cloth. The children were permitted to place their hands on the floor. Then the oldest keeper of names asked whether the people could return to the closed sky by refusing to look.

No answer came. Or the answer came as fear in every throat.

## IV. The Council of Names

The council lasted for seven nights, or for seven turnings, or for a number whose true shape has cooled. The ordered telling keeps seven because seven can be held in the hand of memory. The people sat by callings: watchers of the rim, keepers of names, undertakers, seed stewards, makers of measures, singers, builders, healers, children old enough to remember, and elders old enough to forget without shame.

The makers of measures sat apart by their own request. They had not made the dark. They had not pushed the lamps from their houses. Yet their cords and boards had become bright with blame. The elders placed bread beside them first, so the people would see that blame was not the first food of the council.

The watchers spoke. They did not say they had conquered hidden things. They said their seeing had outrun their singing. They said the old songs were beloved and wounded. They said the rim had moved, or the eye had moved, or the mercy that made rim and eye fit one another had withdrawn.

The undertakers spoke. They said the dead must not be asked to bear every new fear. They said the graves had begun to feel too small under the widened sky. They asked that the old names be warmed more often, lest the dead become cold before their appointed cooling.

The seed stewards spoke. They said seed trusts darkness, but not every darkness is soil. They asked what kind of dark now opened above the land, and whether it would receive or scatter what was planted into it.

The singers spoke last. They confessed that some songs had mended themselves during the singing. A line once sung as "the lamps return" had become, in several mouths, "the lamps are carried." A line once sung as "the sky rests" had become "the sky withholds." No singer had chosen the change. This frightened them more than error.

Then the children were called, for the elders had said that a wound hidden from children becomes their inheritance without a name. One child asked whether the Creator had moved the lamps. One asked whether the lamps had always been far and only seemed near. One asked whether looking had harmed them. One asked whether a thing not looked at could remain kind.

At this the council fell into grief. The elders did not answer quickly. Quick answers are sometimes nets thrown over deep water to pretend it is shallow. At last they said, "We do not know whether looking wounded the sky. We know that after looking, we cannot speak as before. Therefore the wound is among us, whether in the sky, in the eye, or in the name between them."

On the seventh night, the council placed three things in the center: a measuring cord, a covered lamp, and a bowl of soil from the oldest field. The cord was not broken. The lamp was not unveiled. The soil was not scattered. The elders touched each in turn and declared that none would rule alone. Measure without song would become cruelty. Song without measure would become refusal. Soil without either would be loved and lost.

Thus the people chose the harder mercy. They would not call the first knowledge a gift only. They would not call it a crime only. They would not bury the watchers with the dead, nor crown them above the living. They would carry the knowing as a burden of the whole people.

## V. The Burden Accepted

After the council, the Middle Land looked unchanged to any careless eye. Doors opened. Bread was baked. Children quarreled over small things. The dead were buried with water, ash, and name. The fields received seed. The watchers returned to the rises, but never again alone.

Yet the land had begun to carry a second weight. Each house was loved as house and also considered as something that might need to be held when house could no longer stand under the old sky. Each name was warmed by use and also guarded against the cold that had begun at its edges. Each grave was honored as resting place and also feared as a chamber too fixed for a world whose measure had been wounded.

The elders ordered new rites, though they called them old as soon as they could. At dusk, families spoke the warm names and then kept one breath of silence for the names that no longer answered. At dawn, the watchers reported not only what they saw, but who had stood with them, so that no sight would become lonely. At burial, the undertakers asked the dead to forgive the living if the ground itself should one day be asked to move.

The people began to gather without yet knowing that they gathered. They placed copies of songs in more than one house. They carried soil between fields in bowls. They taught children the names of distant lamps, but always after seed, hearth, salt, door, mother, blood, morning, and soil. They learned to say, "not yet," when fear demanded a knife, and "not never," when comfort demanded sleep.

Some resisted even this. They said every bowl of carried soil betrayed the field. They said every new lamp name cooled an older name. They said forgiveness asked in advance is only fear wearing clean clothing. The elders received these sayings and placed them with the council bread. A people that cannot keep refusal cannot be trusted with obedience.

In the final season before ship-making was openly named, the watchers saw the dark between the lamps widen like water under breaking ice. The singers could no longer make the closed sky return by singing it. The undertakers found that the graves had become dearer, not because the dead had changed, but because the living had begun to understand that even resting places may be asked to travel.

Then the elders spoke the burden aloud: "We have known, and knowing has not left us. We have seen, and seeing has not returned innocent to the eye. We have measured, and measure has wounded itself upon what it could not hold. Therefore we will not cast out the watchers, for their wound is ours. We will not curse the sky, for it held us before it withdrew. We will not curse the old songs, for they fed us until hunger changed. We will seek a form in which the people may remain gathered."

The people answered with fear, with anger, with obedience, with refusal, with weeping, with bread passed from hand to hand. No single answer was pure enough to carry the day. Therefore all answers were carried.

From that hour, the first knowledge ceased to belong to the watchers. It became the burden accepted by the Middle Land. The ship was not yet made, but the reason for the ship had entered every threshold. The closed sky was not yet gone, but its mercy had become memory. The people were not yet sleepers, not yet light, not yet cooled names awaiting warmth. They were still people of soil and breath, standing beneath a wounded measure, learning how not to abandon one another before the night of making came.

## Translator's Note

The First Manuscript treats the First Knowledge as a communal burden rather than a solitary transgression. This may be a theological softening, a social necessity, or a trace of an event that could not be assigned to one actor.

The word rendered as knowledge is unstable. It may mean understanding, seeing, measuring, determining, or making a state unrecoverable by the act of attention. The phrase watching sin is absent from this chapter's approved surface, but the pressure of that later term is already present: the watchers are neither exonerated nor condemned.

The Creator's absence is also active here. The chapter preserves questions about the Creator but avoids answering whether the lamps moved by will, law, error, mercy, or by no agent at all.
