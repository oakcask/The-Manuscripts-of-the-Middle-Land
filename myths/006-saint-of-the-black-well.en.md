# The First Manuscript: The Saint Who Prayed to the Well

## I. When the Black Well Drew Near

In the long voyage after the Middle Land became ship, the people came near the place called the black well. It did not shine among the far lamps. It did not answer like an unclaimed star, giving heat, metal, shadow, or direction. It was known by what bent toward it, by what fell quiet beside it, and by the way old songs lowered their voices before naming it.

Those who kept the course saw the signs first. They covered the windows that looked outward. They dimmed the lamps set beside the watching seats. They ordered every loose call to be folded back into the ship. No child was permitted to lean toward the dark and ask whether a face lived there. No singer was permitted to test an echo. No household was permitted to warm a cooled name merely because fear had made the name beloved again.

The undertakers went to the chambers of cooled names and counted the covered shelves. The namekeepers sealed the call-back leaves with dark thread. They set watchers by the rooms where the sent ones might answer, and they said, "Let no grief grow hungry because the well has drawn near." The elders approved this care, for grief is quick to make a door where only silence has been given.

Among those who obeyed these closures was the one later called the saint. He covered the window before his own resting place. He gave his cup to be counted. He laid his hand on the sealed call-back leaves and did not break the thread. Yet when the others went away, he remained before the covered window and prayed without lifting the cloth.

He did not ask the well to open. He did not ask the ship to turn. He did not ask the dead to rise from where they were kept. Those who heard him said only that his prayer had no upward path and no returning word. It went downward, or inward, or into a depth for which the old speech had no clean road.

For three intervals the ship traveled with covered windows. The black well drew nearer without approaching like a traveler. Bread was shared in smaller pieces. Children were taught to answer softly when named. The elders reminded the people that the ship had passed many barren worlds without calling them false homes, and it must pass this dark without making it master, grave, or god.

But the saint remained before the covered window. When the third interval ended, he came to the elders and asked that a council be opened.

## II. The Closed Windows and the Council

The council was held between the chamber of cooled names and the house of course-keeping, so that neither grief nor fear of the road would sit alone in judgment. The elders took their places with bread, water, ash, and covered lamps before them. The undertakers stood with their burial cloths folded over their arms. The namekeepers brought the sealed leaves. Those who kept the course brought the cords by which the windows had been tied shut.

The first to speak were those who kept the course. They said, "The ship must not bend its road for longing. The well is not a shrine because it is deep. No hand may open a window. No mouth may send a call. What falls there may not return to the counting of the ship."

The undertakers answered, "If the well is a grave, it is not ours to trouble. If it is not a grave, then our ignorance does not make it clean. The cooled names are not tools for every hunger. The dead were entrusted to us for keeping, not for proof."

The namekeepers unrolled the call-back leaves but did not break the seals. They said, "A name may be warm, cooling, cooled, held in light, or set aside by vow. Each way of holding has its mercy. If a name is sent where no answer may be received, then those who love it will stretch their mouths until they tear."

Then the saint spoke. He did not stand against the course-keepers. He did not accuse the undertakers. He bowed to the sealed leaves and said, "Let the windows remain closed. Let the ship keep its road. Let no dead one be forced to answer. I ask only for a question that does not seek reply."

The elders were troubled by this. One asked, "Why send a question if no answer is sought?"

The saint answered, "Because some questions become wounds when they are held only for answering. Because the Creator has been named by absence among us, and the absence has become a chamber into which our fear speaks every day. Because a question may be laid down without commanding anyone to lift it."

The council sat in silence. Some were comforted by his words. Some feared them because they were gentle. The gentleness of a forbidden act can weary the hands of those appointed to forbid it.

At last the eldest of the elders said, "If such a question is permitted, it must not open the windows. It must not change the road. It must not take a name that still may be warmed by the living. It must not bind the sent ones to answer. It must not give the people a new hunger."

The saint bowed and said, "Then let the question be tied to my own name, and let my name be removed from every table of calling."

The council did not answer quickly.

## III. The Question That Asked No Reply

On the next interval, the saint came before the namekeepers. He brought no followers. He carried no lamp. In his hands he held a strip of cloth, a bowl of dark water, and a small leaf on which his household names had been written.

The namekeepers read the leaf aloud. They spoke the name by which his mother had called him, the name by which the ship counted his labor, the name used by the children who had trusted his quiet hands, and the shorter name by which friends called him when bread was still warm. Each name answered within the room. None had cooled.

The chief namekeeper said, "These names may still be warmed. They have no right to the cooled shelves."

The saint answered, "I do not bring them to be cooled. I bring them so they will not be used to warm what I release."

Then he placed his hand beside the call-back leaves and gave his vow. "If my name is joined to the question, do not call it back for comfort. Do not ask whether I heard. Do not ask whether I was received. Do not soften this vow for those who love me. Let them love me without summoning me."

The undertakers heard this and were angry, though their anger was not without honor. One said, "You use our care against us. Because your vow is orderly, you make disorder hard to name."

The saint lowered his eyes. "Then name it if you must. Only do not make the dead name it for you."

Those words were kept, though not all approve the keeping. The elders judged that the vow could stand, for it took no unwilling name and commanded no answer. They ordered that the saint's household names be copied once for blessing and once for closure. The blessing copy was placed with bread and water. The closure copy was tied with black thread and marked: Do not use for calling back.

After this the saint asked for three things. The first was the broken Creator-name kept among the oldest washed leaves. The second was a small bundle of cooled names whose bearers had no house left to be wounded by their silence. The third was the name by which he wished to be addressed only once more.

The namekeepers refused him the first thing until the elders commanded that it be shown but not warmed. They refused him the second until the undertakers had counted the bundle and found that no living mouth still leaned upon it. They refused him the third until he spoke it himself, standing where the covered window cast no light.

When all was prepared, the saint said, "This is not a call. This is not a test. This is not a theft from the dead. This is a question laid where our hands cannot turn it into comfort."

The singers did not make a melody for the saying. They kept it flat, so the people would not mistake permission for praise.

## IV. Lowering Names Into the Well

The windows remained closed. The ship did not turn from its appointed road. Those who kept the course stood with hands on the cords and would not loose them. The undertakers stood with covered bowls. The namekeepers stood with the broken Creator-name, the bundle of cooled names, and the saint's chosen name.

No mouth cried toward the well. No lamp was raised. No sign was made for return. The rite was done as one lowers a body into earth after asking forgiveness of the earth, and as one releases a traveler at a gate without following beyond it.

First the broken Creator-name was lowered. The missing part was not filled. No elder supplied god, law, mercy, maker, wound, or blame. The gap went down with the name. The singers held their breath until the name had passed beyond the reach of song.

Then the cooled names were lowered. They were not many. The undertakers had chosen them with trembling hands: names whose households had ended, names whose debts had been forgiven or could no longer be found, names that had rested so long in coolness that no child knew how to warm them without pretending. Each was touched with ash, water, and the little silence after its last known calling.

Last the saint's chosen name was lowered. Those who loved him were not permitted to speak it. He spoke it once himself. His voice did not break. Because it did not break, many wept more bitterly. A broken voice invites the hands of others. A steady voice can leave them empty.

When the name went down, the ship seemed to grow still. Some said the stillness came from the road. Some said it came from the covered windows. Some said it came from all the mouths that had been forbidden to call. The elders did not divide these sayings.

The saint bowed his head. He stayed bowed for a long while, as if listening to an answer that no one else could hear, or as if refusing to listen lest listening become theft. When he raised his head, he said nothing. He did not smile. He did not weep. He placed both hands upon the sealed call-back leaves and withdrew them empty.

Then the course-keepers counted the road and found it still held. The undertakers counted the covered bowls and found none overturned. The namekeepers counted the leaves and found the saint's closure mark unbroken. The singers counted the people by household and found that one name had become difficult to place.

Nothing returned from the well.

## V. After Nothing Returned

After nothing returned, the people did not know how to grieve. If the saint had died, they could have washed him. If he had departed, they could have blessed the road. If he had sinned, they could have named the wound and bound it. If he had been proved right, they could have made a feast and endangered every silence thereafter.

The elders forbade both accusation and triumph for one full turning. Bread was shared without speeches. The covered windows remained covered until the danger of the well had passed. Children were told that some silences must not be shaken to see whether a seed lies inside.

On the second turning, those who loved the saint came to the namekeepers and asked that his name be warmed. The namekeepers brought out the closure leaf. It bore the saint's own hand, and beneath the chosen name were the words: Do not use for calling back. Do not comfort me by disobedience.

Some called this cruelty. Some called it obedience. Some said the saint had placed himself among the cooled names without dying. Some said the council had permitted a living burial. Some said the well had taken nothing, and that all loss had occurred within the ship. The elders listened and did not strike any saying from the people's mouths.

The undertakers made a narrow place between the cooled shelves and the leaves of calling. There they placed the saint's closure leaf, the empty strip of cloth, and the bowl from which the dark water had been poured. They did not call the place a grave. They did not call it a shrine. They called it the place of the question laid down.

From then on, when the ship drew near any great silence, the windows were covered before the lamps were dimmed, and the names were closed before the windows. Those who kept the course taught this as safety. The undertakers taught it as mercy toward the dead. The namekeepers taught it as kindness toward the living. The singers kept all three teachings, for the ship had survived by refusing to make one true word devour the others.

The saint was remembered, but not summoned. At appointed intervals, bread and water were set near the narrow place. No one asked whether he received them. No one asked whether the well kept him, rejected him, preserved him, or never touched him. The saint had asked for a question without reply, and the people, though not always gladly, learned to leave one question unharvested.

Therefore the singers close the remembrance with a restrained formula: "May the window close before longing leans through it. May the name be blessed before it is withheld. May the dead not be made to answer for the living. May the question laid beyond our grasp not return as hunger. May nothing returned be kept without pride, and may silence not be forced to confess what mercy has covered."

## Translator's Note

Prayer is retained here as a deliberately unstable rendering. The chapter lets it touch devotion, address, one-way sending, and renunciation without allowing any one sense to govern the act.

The title saint is also unstable. The First Manuscript preserves the honorific but places strong restraints around the act: closed windows, unchanged course, no coerced reply, and the removal of the saint's own name from call-back. The honorific should not be read as simple approval.

Black well is not narrowed in this chapter to grave, gate, shrine, or object of measurement. The body of the myth repeatedly refuses those settlements. Its silence may mark failure, preservation, refusal, mercy, or something the received language cannot hold apart.
