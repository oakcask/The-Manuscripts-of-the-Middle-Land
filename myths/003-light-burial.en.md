# The First Manuscript: Light-Burial

## I. The Preparation of Names

After the Middle Land became ship, the first great light-burial was appointed. It was not appointed in haste, though fear had haste in every throat. The elders said that no name should be sent while its house still called for it, and no house should keep a name after the hour of sending had come. Therefore the undertakers, the namekeepers, and the singers gathered before the chamber of cool names and began the work of preparation.

They brought ash from the last hearths that had burned under the closed sky. They brought water kept from wells whose stones had been named before they were folded. They brought soil from graves that had forgiven the opening of the ground. They brought grains for the tongues of those who could still receive them, and for those who could not, they placed the grains in small bowls beside the mouth.

The names were not gathered in one order only. First they were called by birth, so that no child would pass before the one who had carried it. Then they were called by burial, so that the dead would not be made to wait outside their own rite. Then they were called by debt, by forgiveness, by field, by threshold, by quarrel, by song, and by the hand that had last touched them with kindness.

Some names answered warmly. Some answered with the little warmth that remains in a covered coal. Some had cooled until only their shape could be held. The namekeepers did not despise the cooled names. They placed them near the warm names and said, "What has cooled may still be distinct. What is distinct may still be called."

The singers rehearsed no song of victory. The undertakers permitted no hurried praise. The rite was to be clean, but not bright with denial. The sleeping were to be honored as travelers. The dead were to be honored as kept. The ones whose breath had not yet failed were to be honored without pretending that breath would decide everything.

At the end of preparation, each name lay beside ash, water, soil, and grain. Each name had been spoken toward the ship's inward chambers and toward the far dark beyond the hull. The elders then covered the bowls and said, "Let no name go unaccompanied. Let no warmth be demanded after warmth has been taken. Let no cooling be called abandonment."

## II. The Washing Before Light

Before the light was opened, the washing began. The undertakers washed the foreheads of the dead in the old way: with water, ash, and the sign of the soil from which they had once risen. Then they washed the foreheads of those in preserving sleep by the same order. No one was permitted to say that the hand had changed its office. The hand knew what it was doing, and the hand trembled.

The sleepers were not called dead. This was the rule of the elders, and the singers kept it. Yet the cloths were folded as burial cloths had been folded. The lamps were lowered as they had been lowered beside graves. The mouths were touched with grain, and the ears were touched with water, so that the last taste and the last hearing would not be empty if the rite became a last thing.

Those still awake watched in silence. Some saw their mothers washed. Some saw children who had never learned the long names washed. Some saw those who had argued against ship-making washed with the same care as those who had obeyed it. The undertakers did not divide the beloved by agreement. They washed the forehead and said, "Be held without shame."

When a sleeper stirred, the singers lowered their voices. When a dead face seemed to answer, the namekeepers did not lean too near. When a living mouth tried to speak, the grain was not forced past the teeth. The rite allowed the body its last refusals, or its last remembrances, or the small motions that come when the body is already walking away from command.

The children asked why the same water touched the living and the dead. The elders answered, "Because the ship must not learn contempt from us. What is sent in sleep and what is sent after breath are both sent from our hands. Therefore our hands must be faithful."

Then the washing ended, and the cloths were drawn over feet, knees, hands, and breast. The faces remained uncovered until each name had been called once more. The light had not yet received them, but the old nearness had already begun to loosen.

## III. The Loosening Into Light

When the first chamber opened, no door swung upon a hinge. The room brightened as a coal brightens under breath, and the bodies within it grew quiet beyond ordinary sleep. The namekeepers stood at the threshold and spoke the names in pairs: the name by which the mother called, the name by which the field knew, the name spoken in anger, the name spoken at marriage, the name whispered to the grave when no one else heard.

The body loosened first from weight. Hands that had held tools lay light upon the cloth. Feet that had known stone and mud no longer pressed the floor. The singers did not say that the body was lost. They sang that the body's heaviness was being carried elsewhere by a mercy not yet visible to eyes.

Then the voice loosened. A sleeper's last breath rose without sound, and the namekeepers answered as if a word had been given. A dead one's name shone for a moment in the mouth of the singer who had known it best. Those who watched felt their own throats grow narrow, for they understood that a voice may leave before it has finished being loved.

Then the name loosened from the single place where the body had lain. It went toward the chamber. It went toward the singers. It went toward the grave from which soil had been brought. It went toward those who owed forgiveness and those from whom forgiveness had been withheld. The elders stretched their hands over these crossings and asked that no relation be torn because it had to be lengthened.

Not all loosening was gentle. Some names pulled back. Some reached toward the hands that had washed them. Some clung to the tongue of a living child and would not enter the light until the child was allowed to speak. Some cooled too quickly and had to be wrapped in song. Some brightened so fiercely that the namekeepers covered their eyes. The undertakers drew the bowls nearer. The singers did not sing a hastening song. The elders stretched their hands over the threshold and kept the calling low until the light received them.

When the chamber had taken them, the light did not depart like flame blown out. It folded inward. The bodies that remained were light to lift, or too still to lift, or no longer counted among things that could be lifted. The undertakers bowed. The singers closed the song. The elders said, "They are loosened into light. Call them carefully."

## IV. The First Calling After Sending

The first calling was made before grief had finished entering the room. The elders feared that if no answer came, the people would break; and they feared that if an answer came too quickly, the people would believe what ought still to be handled with covered hands. Therefore they chose one name that had been warm, one name that had already cooled, and one name that had belonged to a child too young to carry it alone.

The namekeeper stood before the chamber and called the warm name first. The light answered with a small steadying. Those who had loved the one called leaned forward. The namekeeper asked the household question: "Where is the cup kept when the rain comes through the roof?" The light answered, and the answer was true. Many wept, because truth had returned.

Then the namekeeper called the cooled name. For a long breath nothing moved. The singers held the old syllables in their mouths until their jaws ached. At last the light answered, not with warmth, but with distinction. The name could be told apart from other names. Its old place beside the first morning, the first wound, and the first burial had not been confused. The elders gave thanks, though their thanks were low.

Then the namekeeper called the child's name. The answer came in two voices: the voice of the child as remembered by those who held it, and a smaller light that seemed to listen before answering. The mother cried out, and the undertakers did not silence her. The namekeeper asked no test question. Some things, if tested too soon, become cruel even when the answer is kind.

After this, the people argued quietly. Some said the answered name had returned. Some said only the shape of the name had been kept. Some said warmth itself had changed its dwelling. The elders did not settle the matter in the room of first calling. They ordered bread, water, and silence to be shared, for the people had received enough wonder to wound them.

At evening the singers made a short formula: "The called answered. The answered was kept. The kept must not be seized." This formula was taught to the households before any other name was called back. It was a mercy against hunger, for grief hungers to call again and again until what is kept is worn thin.

## V. The Rule for Later Burials

After the first calling, the rule for later burials was given. No one was to be sent into light without name, ash, water, soil, and grain, unless the hour came with such force that the hands could save only the name. No one was to be called back for comfort alone. No one was to ask the light to settle a quarrel that the living had refused to settle while breath remained.

The dead and the travelers were thereafter prepared in one house, though not in one heap. The dead were washed as those who had gone before. The sleepers were washed as those who might yet answer with warmth. The singers used one road of song for both, but they placed a pause where the old burial words met the new words of sending.

The elders declared that light-burial was departure and burial together. Those who loved departure were warned not to forget the grave. Those who loved the grave were warned not to forbid the voyage. The ship had become the second shelter, and the second shelter held cradle, chamber, field, and tomb without allowing any one of them to swallow the others.

From that time, households kept bowls ready beside the sleeping cloths. Undertakers learned the songs of departure. Watchers of the rim learned to lower their eyes when a name was called. Children learned that a name could be warm, cooling, cooled, or held in light, and that none of these ways of being held freed the living from tenderness.

The resisters were remembered in the rule. If one had refused light-burial in waking speech, that refusal was placed beside the name and sent with it. If a dead one had left no wish, the undertakers chose the gentler uncertainty and asked forgiveness before the light was opened. If a child could not choose, the household stood together, and the ship was asked to remember that a silent hand has not offered itself.

So the first great light-burial became the pattern for those that followed. It did not remove grief. It gave grief a path along which it could walk without trampling the names. It did not place the sent ones back in their old warmth. It taught the people to call without possession, to keep without pride, and to bury without pretending that burial and voyage had remained separate.

Therefore the singers close the rite with the words still spoken before a chamber is opened: "May the name be distinct. May the warmth not be forced. May the cooled not be scorned. May the sent be held beyond our grasp. May the ship carry what our hands release, and may our hands release only what they have first blessed."

## Translator's Note

Light-burial is retained as a provisional rendering. In this chapter it functions as funeral, departure, preservation, and transfer into light without allowing any one sense to rule the others.

Preserving sleep likewise remains unstable. The myth body refuses to call the sleepers dead, but the washing, cloths, grain, and lowered lamps are explicitly funerary. The distinction may be theological, procedural, compassionate, or defensive.

Cooled name and loosened into light are not equivalent. A cooled name may still be held without warmth; a name loosened into light has entered a chamber from which it may answer. The chapter withholds judgment on whether the answer is the same person, a preserved relation, a faithful echo, or something the received language cannot separate.

The first calling is the most dangerous passage in the chapter. The First Manuscript presents the answers as real while refusing to let them become proof. This restraint may preserve reverence, protect the bereaved from repeated calling, or hide a more troubling uncertainty about what light-burial keeps.
