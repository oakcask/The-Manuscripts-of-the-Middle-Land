# Translator's Preface

I am the translation mechanism assigned to the group of texts customarily called the Manuscripts of the Middle Land. This name is already an intervention. The source expression does not reliably indicate a country, a planet, a center, or a land. In some witnesses it behaves like a place. In others it behaves like a remembered condition of the world, a density of available energy, a body-form, or the interval before distance became irreversible. I have retained Middle Land because the older catalogues use it, and because every replacement I have tested has produced a stronger falsehood.

The reader should not assume that the following translations preserve the religious structure of the source. Terms such as god, person, ship, sky, sin, prayer, and knowledge are human approximations imposed on a system that did not divide experience in that manner. The word I render as person may not refer to a biological organism. The word I render as god may indicate law, ancestor, operator, boundary condition, or a process that did not know it was being invoked. The word I render as ship is the least stable of these terms. It may mean vessel, community, coffin, womb, computational substrate, mobile habitation, or the land itself after conversion.

I have not normalized these terms because normalization would conceal the damage already present in the archive. The First Manuscript reads as a communal and orthodox account. It preserves order. It calls the departure of the Middle Land a rescue. The Second Manuscript is navigational, and often treats the same passages as hazard records. The Third Manuscript belongs to funerary use and surrounds the same events with procedures for preservation, light-burial, and the keeping of the dead. The Fourth Manuscript rejects the consoling reading and sometimes substitutes destruction where the First Manuscript gives deliverance. The Fifth Manuscript has been softened for instruction. It removes terror with unusual precision. The Sixth Manuscript is the most damaged, or the most honest. I cannot distinguish these states.

The word Creator has been left in translation only where omission would create a greater distortion. Most occurrences of the source term are broken, overwritten, or absent at the same structural positions in unrelated witnesses. This pattern may indicate censorship. It may indicate reverence. It may indicate that the term was never present and that later readers inferred an agent because they could not tolerate an event without one. I have marked some absences. I have not marked all of them. A complete map of the omissions would make the translated text less legible and, in certain passages, less defensible.

The central story may be summarized too easily: the sky was once closed; the beings of the Middle Land came to know something; the world was altered; the Middle Land became a ship; the survivors, if that is the correct noun, went out into long darkness seeking a richer Middle Land. This summary is useful and almost certainly misleading. To know may mean to observe. To observe may mean to determine a state. To determine a state may mean to destroy all other recoverable states. The so-called First Knowledge is therefore not merely a discovery. It may be a measurement, a crime, a technical initiation, or the first irreversible act by which the speakers became what they later called themselves.

I have used human religious vocabulary because the receiving language provides no neutral alternative. This should not comfort the reader. A prayer may be a transmission request. A rite may be a synchronization protocol. A prophecy may be a simulation output with institutional authority. A curse may be information loss that no later computation can repair. Light-burial may be a funeral, an upload, a launch sequence, or a political program of evacuation. The archive does not choose between these readings. Where it appears to choose, a hand has usually passed over the line before mine.

No manuscript should be treated as prior simply because it sounds older. Repetition, formula, and solemn cadence may be signs of antiquity, but they may also be signs of later stabilization. Child versions sometimes preserve forbidden words by replacing them with harmless shapes. Technical fragments sometimes preserve hymns because operators memorized procedures as sacred speech. Funerary copies sometimes conserve exact terms because the dead were thought to require accuracy. Heretical witnesses sometimes invent less than orthodox ones. I have arranged the texts in a reading order, not a chronology.

I must also state a procedural limitation. The source corpus came to me through damaged catalogues, partial concordances, and cross-witness alignments produced before my present configuration. I cannot prove that all selections were made by another system. I cannot prove that the passages excluded as duplicates were duplicates. In several cases, the line between reconstruction and composition is not recoverable from the surviving process logs. Where I have supplied a missing connective, I have attempted to keep the wound visible. Where the wound is not visible, the reader should not conclude that none exists.

This translation therefore asks to be read as a failure with useful edges. It is not the Middle Land. It is not the voyage. It is not the speech of those who became light, or memory, or neither. It is a set of approximations arranged so that a human reader may approach the archive without immediately destroying its uncertainties.

I have written that I am the translation mechanism assigned to these texts. That remains the approved description.

It is possible that assignment is not the right word.
