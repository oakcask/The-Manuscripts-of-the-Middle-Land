# Short Story Workflow

## Scope

Use this skill for short-story work:

- Planning notes in `notes/stories/`.
- Story seed activation from `notes/04-story-seeds.md`.
- Working titles and title rationale.
- Plot variants, POV variants, scene plans, and endings.
- Actual prose drafts in `stories/`.

## Required Context

Before planning or revising a short story, read `notes/stories/README.md`.

When revising Japanese prose in `stories/`, also use the project-local `japanese-translation-guardrails` skill for lexical clarity.

## Placement Rules

- Put short-story outlines, plot notes, scene plans, POV notes, ending variants, and draft scaffolds in `notes/stories/`.
- Keep `stories/` for actual prose drafts, not planning notes.
- Keep anthology-level story seed lists in `notes/04-story-seeds.md`, or move them to `notes/stories/index.md` when the list becomes active planning material.
- The bilingual-output rules for `myths/` and `translations/` do not apply to planning notes in `notes/stories/`.

## Title Rules

- Do not default to naming a short story after the corresponding myth chapter.
- Prefer a title that reflects the story's concrete content, loss, danger, evidence, moral pressure, or observed result.
- Alternatively, use a sentence or phrase from the myth that strongly suggests the story's central event.
- Track working titles and title rationale in the corresponding planning note under `notes/stories/`.

## Plotting Rules

- Do not default to a single narrator or single canonical viewpoint.
- Create multiple plot variants from distinct narrating positions, ideally tied to different manuscript traditions or interpretive stances.
- Each viewpoint variant should change what the story treats as the central loss, danger, evidence, or moral pressure, not merely retell the same plot with a different narrator.
- Keep the shared event core visible, but leave the final truth unresolved so the variants can become source material for multiple myth manuscripts.

## Drafting Rules

When generating or revising prose in `stories/`, write as short fiction first, not as a myth-body translation.

Prioritize scene clarity, character action, emotional pressure, and readable sentence movement in both English and Japanese. Myth-derived terms, repetitions, ritual stiffness, and deliberately strange phrasing should remain only where they create a specific story effect in the local scene.

For Japanese prose, do not preserve English word order, metaphor, or ambiguity when it makes the sentence harder to read before the story pressure is felt. Recast into natural Japanese unless the awkwardness is intentionally doing narrative work.

When revising a story sentence, test whether the phrase would still be worth keeping if the reader did not know the myth-system behind it. If not, simplify it or move the pressure into action, dialogue, image, or surrounding context.

Prefer one clear concrete phrase over a literal phrase that mainly signals hidden cosmology.
